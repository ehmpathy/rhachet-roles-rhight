# ref.insurance-unpermitted-work-discovery

## .what

how insurance companies discover unpermitted work, determine liability, and the legal precedents for claim denials.

## .why this matters

- insurers typically discover unpermitted work at claim time, not policy issuance
- buyers inherit liability for previous owners' unpermitted work
- standard home inspections do NOT verify permit status
- case law establishes when coverage exists despite unpermitted work

---

## .how insurers discover unpermitted work

### at claim time (primary discovery)

insurers typically do NOT check permits when you buy a policy. discovery happens when you file a claim and they have financial incentive to investigate.

| method | description |
|--------|-------------|
| permit database search | public records query to county/city permit system |
| assessor records comparison | tax records vs. actual structure square footage |
| aerial imagery comparison | timestamped satellite photos show when additions appeared |
| physical inspection | adjusters identify non-code-compliant installation patterns |
| SIU investigation | forensic analysis, interviews, surveillance for suspicious claims |
| contractor interviews | repair contractors reveal prior unpermitted modifications |
| MLS listing archives | Redfin/Zillow historical photos show condition at sale |

| method | how it works |

## .does liability transfer to new owners?

**YES.** the current owner is responsible regardless of who performed the work.

> "When you buy a home, you assume its liabilities, including any outstanding fines from unpermitted work, and you're also responsible for bringing the property up to code."
> — Rocket Mortgage

### what helps if previous owner did the work

- you disclosed unpermitted status to insurer before loss
- loss is unrelated to the unpermitted modification
- work was done to code, just not permitted
- you are in process of retroactive permit

### what does NOT protect you

- "as-is" purchase (transfers physical risk, not disclosure obligations)
- home inspection passed (inspectors don't check permits)
- title insurance (covers title defects, not code compliance)
- ignorance (liability attaches to property, not knowledge)

## .title insurance and unpermitted work

### standard title insurance does NOT cover unpermitted work

title insurance covers ownership defects (liens, encumbrances, disputes) — not physical condition or code compliance.

### enhanced ALTA policies may help

> ALTA Homeowners policies provide **Covered Risk 18**: coverage when you are "ordered by a State or Municipal authority to remove or remedy" structures "built without obtaining a building permit."
> — Stewart Title

coverage is subject to deductibles and maximum liability limits.

## .home inspection limitations

standard home inspections explicitly EXCLUDE permit verification.

> "Inspectors are not required to identify code compliance, permit status, or the presence of environmental hazards like mold, asbestos, or lead-based paint unless specifically contracted."
> — InterNACHI Standards of Practice

| # | source | key fact |
|---|--------|----------|
| 8 | InterNACHI Standards of Practice | permit verification NOT included in standard inspection |

**implication**: a "passed" inspection does NOT mean work is permitted.

## .case law: when coverage exists despite unpermitted work

### concurrent causation doctrine

**Sebo v. American Home Assurance Co., 208 So. 3d 694 (Fla. 2016)**
- Florida Supreme Court
- when independent perils converge (defects + covered perils like storms), coverage may exist if policy lacks anti-concurrent causation language
- **application**: even when unpermitted work contributes to damage, coverage may exist if a covered peril is also involved

### efficient proximate cause doctrine

**Garvey v. State Farm Fire & Casualty Co., 48 Cal.3d 395 (Cal. 1989)**
- California Supreme Court
- when negligent construction and excluded perils are concurrent causes, the "efficient proximate cause" determines coverage
- **application**: unpermitted construction negligence may be covered if it's the efficient proximate cause

**Sabella v. Wisler, 59 Cal.2d 21 (Cal. 1963)**
- California Supreme Court
- established efficient proximate cause doctrine
- third-party negligence (not excluded "settling") was efficient proximate cause
- **application**: if unpermitted work negligence sets chain of events in motion, coverage may exist

### ensuing loss clauses

**Vision One, LLC v. Philadelphia Indemnity Insurance Co., 174 Wash.2d 501 (Wash. 2012)**
- Washington Supreme Court
- ensuing loss provisions preserve coverage even when faulty workmanship is excluded
- **application**: unpermitted work may be excluded, but ensuing damage from covered events (collapse, water) may be covered

### construction defect coverage

**Ewing Construction Co. v. Amerisure Insurance Co., 420 S.W.3d 30 (Tex. 2014)**
- Texas Supreme Court
- faulty workmanship claims may constitute an "occurrence" under liability policies
- **application**: defective unpermitted work may still trigger liability coverage

## .case law: when coverage is denied

### faulty workmanship exclusions

**Saunders v. Florida Peninsula Insurance Co.**
- Florida Third District Court of Appeal
- faulty workmanship exclusion applied when contractor left roof exposed
- **application**: unpermitted roofing work causing water damage may be excluded

### multiple causes analysis

**Julian v. Hartford Underwriters Insurance Co., 35 Cal.4th 747 (Cal. 2005)**
- California Supreme Court
- examined how multiple potential causes interact with policy exclusions
- **application**: when unpermitted grading contributes to slope failure, courts analyze multiple causes

---

## .case law: coverage timing and triggers

**Montrose Chemical Corp. v. Admiral Insurance Co., 10 Cal.4th 645 (Cal. 1995)**
- California Supreme Court
- continuous injury triggers all policies in effect during damage periods
- **application**: ongoing damage from unpermitted work may trigger multiple policy periods

**Murray v. State Farm Fire & Casualty Co., 219 Cal.App.3d 58 (Cal. 1990)**
- California Court of Appeal
- addressed installation defects causing pipe damage
- **application**: improperly installed unpermitted plumbing illustrates causation disputes

**Pardee Construction Co. v. Insurance Company of the West, 77 Cal.App.4th 1340 (Cal. 2000)**
- California Court of Appeal
- addressed duty to defend in construction defect cases
- **application**: relevant when unpermitted subcontractor work causes claims

## .bottom line

**discovery**: insurers find unpermitted work at claim time via permit searches, aerial imagery, and physical inspection — not at policy issuance.

**liability transfer**: YES, buyers inherit liability. home inspections don't check permits. title insurance doesn't cover it. ignorance doesn't protect you.

**coverage depends on**:
- policy language (anti-concurrent causation clauses)
- state doctrine (efficient proximate cause vs. concurrent causation)
- ensuing loss provisions
- whether covered peril also contributed to damage
