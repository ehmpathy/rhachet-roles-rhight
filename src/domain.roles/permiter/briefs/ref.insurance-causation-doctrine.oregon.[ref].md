# ref.insurance-causation-doctrine.oregon

## .what

Oregon applies the **efficient proximate cause (EPC) doctrine** for property damage claims with mixed causes.

## .the rule

the EPC is "the active and efficient cause that sets in motion a chain of events which bring about a result without the intervention of any force, starting and actively from a new and independent source."

when multiple causes contribute to a single loss, the EPC determines coverage.

## .key case law

**Farmers Insurance Co. of Oregon v. Trutanich, 123 Or. App. 6, 858 P.2d 1332 (1993)**
- Oregon Court of Appeals established EPC framework
- example: if earthquake leads to dam collapse, then flood — earthquake (not flood) is the cause of loss

**Oregon Supreme Court Construction Defect Rulings**
- property damage from construction defect CAN be caused by an "occurrence" under CGL policies
- contractor tort liability for defective work may constitute a covered "occurrence"
- BUT no duty to indemnify when liability arises **solely** from breach of contract (those damages are not accidental)

## .how Oregon handles mixed causation

courts look at what "set in motion" the chain of events:

| EPC is... | coverage? |
|-----------|-----------|
| covered peril (storm, fire) | YES — even if excluded perils also contributed |
| excluded peril (construction defect) | NO |
| unclear / multiple independent causes | analyze each |

**key point**: construction defects CAN serve as the efficient proximate cause of damage.

## .anti-concurrent causation clauses

Oregon is NOT among states that refuse to enforce ACC clauses (CA, ND, WA, WV do refuse). however, Oregon's strong EPC doctrine provides policyholder protections via focus on predominant cause.

| statute | rule |
|---------|------|
| ORS 12.110(1) | tort claims for property injury: 2 years from discovery |
| ORS 12.135 | construction claims: 10 years from substantial completion |

## .practical implication for unpermitted work

| scenario | likely outcome |
|----------|----------------|
| storm (covered) set chain in motion, unpermitted work contributed | coverage likely |
| unpermitted work set chain in motion, storm contributed | denial likely |
| unpermitted work directly caused damage | denial |
| damage unrelated to unpermitted work | coverage |

**contract vs. tort distinction:**
- if claim sounds only in contract (breach of warranty) → coverage unlikely
- if claim sounds in tort (negligence caused unintended damage) → coverage may exist

**action items:**
- analyze what "set in motion" the damage chain
- if weather event was the EPC, coverage arguments are stronger
- if construction defect was the EPC, expect denial
