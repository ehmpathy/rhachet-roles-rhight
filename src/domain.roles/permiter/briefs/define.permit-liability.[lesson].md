# define.permit-liability

## .what

a permit is a legal document that assigns liability. whoever pulls the permit certifies code compliance and accepts responsibility for defects, fines, and injuries.

## .why

- permit = liability assignment, not just paperwork
- who pulls determines who pays when things go wrong **during the process**
- once permit is issued and inspection passes, protection is similar
- owner-builder status shifts risk during construction, not after approval

---

## key distinction: pre-approval vs post-approval

**the liability difference is primarily DURING the permit process, not after.**

| phase | contractor-pulled | homeowner-pulled |
|-------|-------------------|------------------|
| **pre-approval** | contractor bears risk | YOU bear risk |
| **post-approval** | permit on record | permit on record |
| **insurance claim** | protected | protected |

### what "pre-approval risk" means

| risk | contractor-pulled | homeowner-pulled |
|------|-------------------|------------------|
| inspection fails, needs fixes | contractor pays | YOU pay |
| worker injured during corrections | contractor's insurance | YOU (personally) |
| technical questions from inspector | contractor answers | YOU must answer |
| fines for code violations found | contractor pays | YOU pay |

### what happens AFTER approval

once the permit is issued and inspection passes:

| factor | contractor-pulled | homeowner-pulled |
|--------|-------------------|------------------|
| permit on file | YES | YES |
| inspection record | YES | YES |
| insurance protection | YES | YES |
| proof of code compliance | YES | YES |

**bottom line**: the permit provides the same protection either way once approved. the difference is who bears the cost and risk of GETTING to approval.

---

## the permit as liability contract

a permit says:

> "I certify this work will meet code. I accept responsibility if it doesn't."

the permit applicant becomes the **responsible party** for:

| responsibility | what it means |
|----------------|---------------|
| code compliance | work must meet NEC/UPC/IMC standards |
| inspection passage | must answer inspector's technical questions |
| defect correction | must fix violations at own expense |
| fines and penalties | must pay if code violated |
| injury liability | may be liable if someone is hurt |

---

## who pulls = who is liable

| who pulls permit | who answers to inspector | who pays to fix defects | who pays fines | who is liable for injuries |
|------------------|-------------------------|------------------------|----------------|---------------------------|
| **contractor** | contractor | contractor | contractor | contractor's insurance |
| **homeowner** | you | you | you | you (maybe uninsured) |

> "A permit makes the contractor legally responsible for their portion of the work."
> — [NYC Buildings (gov)](https://www.nyc.gov/site/buildings/dob/project-requirements-contractor-permit-and-insurance.page)

> "If no permit is obtained, you (the owner) are legally responsible."
> — [Miami-Dade County (gov)](https://wwwx.miamidade.gov/global/economy/building/contractor-owner-builder-responsibilities.page)

---

## contractor-pulled permit

when a licensed contractor pulls the permit:

| factor | outcome |
|--------|---------|
| code compliance | contractor certifies |
| inspector questions | contractor answers |
| failed inspection | contractor fixes at their expense |
| defect causes damage | contractor's insurance covers |
| worker injured | contractor's workers comp covers |
| fines | contractor pays |

> "The contractor who performed the work will be responsible for bringing the project into compliance at his or her expense, including any penalties imposed by the building department."
> — [Miami-Dade County (gov)](https://wwwx.miamidade.gov/global/economy/building/contractor-owner-builder-responsibilities.page)

**contractor insurance requirements**:

| coverage | purpose |
|----------|---------|
| general liability | covers property damage, injuries |
| workers compensation | covers employee injuries |
| professional liability | covers errors in work |

> "Licensed contractors must always have general liability and worker's compensation insurance in effect."
> — [NYC Buildings (gov)](https://www.nyc.gov/site/buildings/dob/project-requirements-contractor-permit-and-insurance.page)

---

## homeowner-pulled permit (owner-builder)

when you pull the permit yourself, you become an **owner-builder**:

| factor | outcome |
|--------|---------|
| code compliance | YOU certify |
| inspector questions | YOU answer |
| failed inspection | YOU fix at your expense |
| defect causes damage | YOUR insurance (maybe) |
| worker injured | YOU may be liable |
| fines | YOU pay |

> "An owner-builder assumes responsibility for the overall job which may include, but is not limited to, taxes, insurance, and other legal liabilities."
> — [Ventura County (gov)](https://rma.venturacounty.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/considering-becoming-an-owner-builder-handout.pdf)

---

## owner-builder insurance gap

standard homeowner's insurance does NOT cover owner-builder activities:

| coverage type | homeowner policy | owner-builder status |
|---------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| fire, theft, liability (normal) | YES | YES |
| construction accidents | NO | NO |
| injury to workers you hire | NO | YOU if uninsured |
| defects in permitted work | depends | likely excluded |

> "Course of Construction and Builder's Risk types of insurance contracts DO NOT INCLUDE liability coverage. You'll need to secure a separate General Liability policy."
> — [UBuildIt](https://www.ubuildit.com/insurance-for-owner-builders/)

### the worker injury scenario

| who pulls permit | worker falls off ladder | who pays |
|------------------|------------------------|----------|
| contractor | contractor's workers comp | contractor's insurance |
| homeowner | worker sues you | YOU (personally) |

if you hire an unlicensed or uninsured worker while you act as owner-builder, YOU become their de facto employer for liability purposes.

---

## why contractors should pull retroactive permits

for inherited work you didn't perform:

| approach | who certifies | who is liable | cost |
|----------|--------------|---------------|------|
| you pull permit | you | you | permit fee only |
| contractor pulls | contractor | contractor | $150-400 + permit |

**the problem with homeowner-pulled retroactive permit**:

you certify that someone else's work meets code. if inspection fails:
- you pay to fix defects
- you pay fines
- you pay re-inspection fees
- you answer technical questions you may not understand

**the contractor-pulled approach**:

contractor inspects the work, certifies it meets code, pulls the permit. if inspection fails:
- contractor pays to fix defects
- contractor pays fines
- contractor handles re-inspection
- contractor answers technical questions

**once approved**: both approaches provide the same permit protection.

---

## liability scenarios

### scenario 1: inspector finds defect

| who pulled | what happens | who pays |
|------------|--------------|----------|
| contractor | contractor fixes, schedules re-inspection | contractor |
| homeowner | you hire someone to fix, you schedule re-inspection | you |

### scenario 2: permitted work causes fire 5 years later

| who pulled | insurance claim | outcome |
|------------|-----------------|---------|
| contractor | covered peril + permit on file | paid |
| homeowner | covered peril + permit on file | paid |

**note**: once permit is issued and inspection passed, both have the same protection. the permit record proves code compliance regardless of who pulled it.

### scenario 3: worker injured on your property

| who pulled | worker status | who is liable |
|------------|---------------|---------------|
| contractor | contractor's employee | contractor's workers comp |
| homeowner | hired by you as owner-builder | YOU if worker uninsured |

---

## the $150-400 question

| option | cost | pre-approval liability |
|--------|------|------------------------|
| pull permit yourself | $100-350 (permit only) | you bear all process risk |
| contractor pulls | $250-750 (permit + certification) | contractor bears process risk |

**pre-approval risk calculation**:

| exposure (before approval) | potential cost |
|----------------------------|----------------|
| failed inspection corrections | $500-5,000 |
| fines for code violations | $500-10,000 |
| worker injury (if you hire help) | $50,000-500,000+ |

**post-approval**: both options provide the same permit protection.

$150-400 to transfer pre-approval risk = rational for complex or inherited work.

---

## when homeowner-pull makes sense

| scenario | homeowner-pull OK? |
|----------|-------------------|
| you did the work yourself | YES (you know it meets code) |
| simple work (water heater, outlet) | YES (low risk) |
| you're a licensed tradesperson | YES (you can certify) |
| inherited work from previous owner | NO (you can't certify their work) |
| complex work (panel, rewire, HVAC) | NO (high risk) |
