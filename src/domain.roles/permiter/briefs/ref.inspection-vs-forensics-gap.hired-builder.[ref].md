# ref.inspection-vs-forensics-gap.hired-builder

## .what

when a licensed contractor performs electrical work WITH permit and inspection, fire forensics can still identify workmanship defects that passed inspection. the key difference: subrogation allows insurer recovery from contractor.

## .why

- permit approval does not guarantee defect-free work
- forensics can find defects invisible at inspection
- contractor's insurance provides subrogation target
- homeowner's claim is paid, then insurer recovers

---

## tl;dr

| question | answer |
|----------|--------|
| is permitted contractor work covered if defect causes fire? | YES — ensuing loss doctrine |
| what happens after claim paid? | subrogation against contractor |
| who ultimately pays? | contractor's insurance |
| homeowner outcome | made whole |

## case law: coverage for permitted work with defects

### Fruchthandler v. Tri-State Consumer Insurance Co. (NY 2019)

**citation**: 2019 NY Slip Op 02502 (App. Div. 2d Dept. 2019)

**facts**: fire in two-family home. insurer denied based on faulty workmanship exclusion — "improper conditions" in junction box.

**ruling**: coverage GRANTED. ensuing loss exception applied.

**reasoning**: fire occurred 2 years after faulty work, caused damage "wholly separate from the defective property itself."

**relevance**: establishes defect → fire → coverage under ensuing loss.

**source**: [NY Appellate Digest](https://www.newyorkappellatedigest.com/2019/04/03/the-exception-to-the-faulty-workmanship-exclusion-in-the-fire-insurance-policy-applied-to-preserve-coverage-for-ensuing-loss-second-dept/)

### Gardens Condominium v. Farmers Insurance (WA 2024)

**citation**: 2024 WL (Wash. 2024)

**ruling**: faulty workmanship → condensation → damage = COVERED. ensuing loss preserved coverage.

**source**: [Justia](https://law.justia.com/cases/washington/supreme-court/2024/101-892-4.html)

## subrogation case law: insurer recovery from contractor

### Fireman's Fund v. Triton Subs — $7.8M verdict

**facts**: electrical fire destroyed Quiznos store (Monterey, CA). contractor failed to encase cable in protective conduit. cable damaged at installation, carbonized over 3 years, then ignited.

**timeline**:
| event | time |
|-------|------|
| work performed | day 0 |
| inspection passed | day 1 |
| fire | 3 years later |

**claim**: Fireman's Fund paid $7M to policyholders.

**subrogation**: sued Triton (who selected contractor).

**verdict**: 12-0 on liability, 10-2 on damages. $7.8M judgment.

**key detail**: contractor was "low bid" at 50% less than experienced contractors.

**source**: [Jury Verdict Alert](https://juryverdictalert.com/insurance-subrogation-recovery-indemnity/insurance-company-v-contractor)

### Sorority Chapter Fire — $1.38M subrogation

**facts**: fire on top floor/roof of chapter facility. total claim: $1,379,877.

**investigation**: electrical engineer found electrician knocked wiring loose while on site.

**outcome**: insurer recovered from electrician's insurance.

**source**: [MJ Sorority - Subrogation Case Study](https://mjsorority.com/resource/property-case-study-subrogation-example/)

### Restaurant Panel Fire — $180K + 6 weeks closure

**facts**: electrician upgraded 200-amp panel. 8 weeks later, loose connection → arc flash → fire. $180K damage + business interruption.

**investigation**: forensics identified under-torqued connection.

**subrogation**: insurer recovered from electrician's "completed operations" coverage.

**source**: [Landesblosch - Electrical Contractor Insurance](https://www.landesblosch.com/blog/electrical-contractor-insurance)

## the subrogation mechanism

### step-by-step process

| step | action | who |
|------|--------|-----|
| 1 | fire occurs | — |
| 2 | homeowner files claim | homeowner |
| 3 | insurer pays claim | homeowner's insurer |
| 4 | forensic investigation | origin/cause expert |
| 5 | defect identified | electrical engineer |
| 6 | demand letter sent | insurer → contractor |
| 7 | contractor's insurance responds | contractor's GL policy |
| 8 | recovery | insurer recoups payout |

### why this protects homeowners

| factor | benefit |
|--------|---------|
| claim paid first | you're not held up by litigation |
| subrogation is insurer's problem | you're out of it |
| contractor's insurance pays | not you |
| no premium impact (usually) | recovery offsets loss |

**source**: [Dick Law Firm - Subrogation Claims](https://www.dicklawfirm.com/blog/2025/may/what-is-a-subrogation-claim-/)

## contractor insurance that enables recovery

### required coverages

| coverage | what it covers |
|----------|----------------|
| general liability | third-party injury/damage |
| completed operations | defects found after work done |
| workers compensation | employee injuries |

### completed operations is critical

| scenario | coverage |
|----------|----------|
| fire while work in progress | general liability |
| fire 8 weeks after | completed operations |
| fire 3 years after | completed operations |

> "Completed operations coverage is critical for electricians because electrical fires and installation failures can occur weeks or months after the work was done."

**source**: [The Hartford - Electrical Contractor Insurance](https://www.thehartford.com/business-insurance/electrical-contractor-insurance)

## comparison: owner-builder vs hired-builder

### post-fire outcome

| factor | owner-builder | hired-builder |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| claim covered? | YES (ensuing loss) | YES (ensuing loss) |
| subrogation target | NONE | contractor |
| insurer recovery | NONE | from contractor |
| your outcome | claim paid | claim paid |
| insurer outcome | absorbs loss | recovers loss |

### the real difference

both are COVERED under ensuing loss doctrine when fire is the covered peril.

the difference is WHO PAYS:
- owner-builder: insurer absorbs loss (no recovery)
- hired-builder: contractor's insurance pays (via subrogation)

## waiver of subrogation caution

### what it is

some contracts waive insurer's right to subrogate:

> "A waiver of subrogation prevents an insurance company from recovery from a named party, even if that named party was negligent."

**source**: [MWL Law](https://www.mwl-law.com/subrogation-waiver-prevents-insured-from-recovering-deductible-court-enforces-waiver-of-subrogation-despite-no-insurance-payout/)

### when it matters

| contract type | waiver common? |
|---------------|----------------|
| residential electrical | rare |
| commercial construction | common |
| general contractor | common |

### recommendation

avoid waiver of subrogation in residential contracts — it eliminates the recovery path that makes hired contractors valuable.

## key takeaways

| question | answer |
|----------|--------|
| is permitted contractor work covered? | YES — ensuing loss doctrine |
| what happens to contractor? | subrogation against their insurance |
| homeowner impact? | made whole, no further involvement |
| why hire licensed contractor? | subrogation recovery path |

**bottom line**: 
- fire from permitted work is covered (same as owner-builder)
- the VALUE of a contractor is the subrogation target
- insurer recovers from contractor's insurance
- this is why license and insurance requirements exist
