# hazard.subtle-unpermitted-work

## .hazard

invisible unpermitted work (electrical, plumbing, HVAC) creates latent insurance claim denial risk that surfaces only at claim time.

## .why it matters

- most homebuyers inherit unpermitted work without knowing
- standard inspections do NOT check permits
- title insurance does NOT cover unpermitted work
- insurers discover unpermitted work when you file a claim — then deny it

## .the exposure

```
unpermitted panel sits quietly for 10 years
    ↓
fire occurs
    ↓
adjuster searches permit database
    ↓
no permit found
    ↓
claim denied
```

## .common subtle unpermitted work

| work | visibility | insurance risk |
|------|------------|----------------|
| electrical panel upgrade | NONE | fire claim denied |
| rewired circuits | NONE | fire claim denied |
| water heater replacement | LOW | flood/fire claim denied |
| plumbing pipes | NONE | leak/flood claim denied |
| HVAC replacement | LOW | fire/CO claim denied |
| bathroom remodel | LOW | water damage claim denied |
| kitchen remodel | LOW | fire claim denied |

## .why it's hard to detect

- no square footage change
- no exterior change
- no aerial imagery evidence
- passes standard home inspection
- seller may not know (prior owner did it)
- seller may not disclose

## .protection

**before purchase:**
1. pull complete permit history from building department
2. compare permit dates to equipment ages
3. hire trade-specific inspectors if gaps found

**after purchase:**
- disclose known unpermitted work to insurer
- pursue retroactive permits where possible
- consult attorney about coverage implications

## .how often does this happen?

### the nuanced reality

**denials for unpermitted work are RARE — but they do happen.**

insurance professionals on forums consistently say they rarely see denials purely for lack of permits. however, documented cases exist where claims were denied when unpermitted work **directly caused** the damage.

### what insurance adjusters actually say

| source | quote |
|--------|-------|

| michael_wilkins (Claims Adjuster) | "No policy language excludes code-related causes of loss." |
| whalemania_ (Insurance Adjuster, CT) | "Insurance policies cover stupidity... Homeowners claims caused by covered losses are paid regardless of licensing." |
| marksr (DIY forum moderator) | "while an insurance company might be able to deny a claim caused by faulty unpermitted work, they rarely do so." |

source: Fine Homebuilding forum, DoItYourself.com forum

### documented denial cases

**Bridgewater, NJ case** (cited on Houzz forum):
> "A few years ago, after an electrical fire burned down a house in Bridgewater, the insurance company denied a claim filed by the homeowners after finding that no permits had been taken out for the finished basement where the fire started."

**Michael Thomas case** (DoItYourself forum):
> claim "denied due to construction defect" when unpermitted room additions included cut/modified trusses, unsafe electrical work, plumbing work, and tampered gas lines

### when denials actually happen

| scenario | outcome |
|----------|---------|
| unpermitted work **directly caused** damage | denial likely |
| damage **unrelated** to unpermitted work | coverage likely |
| **both** covered peril AND unpermitted work contributed | depends on state doctrine |
| construction defects involved | denial likely |
| work was unpermitted but done to code | coverage likely |

### the mixed causation problem

when BOTH a covered peril (fire, storm) AND unpermitted work contributed to damage, outcome depends on state law:

| doctrine | states | rule |
|----------|--------|------|
| concurrent causation | Florida | coverage may exist if policy lacks anti-concurrent language |
| efficient proximate cause | California, Oregon | what "set in motion" the chain determines coverage |
| ensuing loss | Washington | damage RESULTING from covered event may be covered |

**implication:**
- pure causation by unpermitted work → denied
- pure causation by covered peril → covered  
- mixed causation → **litigation territory**

### why this hazard is not louder

the hazard is real but rare. most claims get paid. the risk is:
- **tail risk**: low probability, high consequence
- **causation matters**: coverage depends on what caused the damage (see case law below)
- **no systematic data**: insurers don't publish denial statistics

### case law establishing causation principle

| case | holding |
|------|---------|
| Sebo v. American Home, 208 So. 3d 694 (Fla. 2016) | coverage exists if covered peril also involved |
| Garvey v. State Farm, 48 Cal.3d 395 (Cal. 1989) | "efficient proximate cause" determines coverage |
| Sabella v. Wisler, 59 Cal.2d 21 (Cal. 1963) | third-party negligence can be covered cause |
| Vision One v. Philadelphia Indemnity, 174 Wash.2d 501 (Wash. 2012) | ensuing loss clauses preserve coverage |

**implication**: courts look at WHAT caused the damage, not just whether unpermitted work existed. if a covered peril (fire, storm) also contributed, coverage may exist despite unpermitted work.

### the actual risk profile

| unpermitted work type | risk level | why |
|-----------------------|------------|-----|
| electrical (panel, circuits) | HIGHER | fire causation is traceable |
| plumbing | MODERATE | leak causation is traceable |
| structural (walls, trusses) | HIGHER | collapse/defect causation |
| cosmetic (finishes, fixtures) | LOW | rarely causes covered losses |
| HVAC | MODERATE | fire/CO causation possible |

## .bottom line

assume any home has unpermitted work until you verify otherwise. the cost of permit research ($100-300) is trivial compared to a denied claim ($50k-500k+).
