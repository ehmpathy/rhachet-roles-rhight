# howto.detect-unpermitted-work

## .what

methods for homebuyers to detect unpermitted work before purchase.

## .why

- standard home inspections do NOT verify permit status
- title insurance does NOT cover unpermitted work
- buyers inherit liability regardless of who performed the work
- detection before purchase enables negotiation or walk-away

---

## .permit search methods

### online permit portals

most cities and counties maintain searchable permit databases.

**how to find**: search "[city/county] permit search" or "[city/county] building department online portal"

**what records show**:
- permit type and scope of work approved
- application date
- inspection pass/fail dates
- final sign-off status
- contractor information

### request records directly

visit or call the local building department with the property address.

- request complete permit history file
- small fee may apply for document copies
- ask specifically: "what permits exist for this address?"

### what to look for

| record | indicates |
|--------|-----------|
| permit exists, final inspection passed | work was permitted and approved |
| permit exists, no final inspection | work started but never completed/inspected |
| no permit for visible work | unpermitted modification |
| permit scope vs. actual work | scope creep beyond what was approved |

## .physical signs of unpermitted work

### visual indicators

| sign | what it suggests |
|------|------------------|
| inconsistent construction quality | different work done at different times/standards |
| non-standard electrical panel | DIY or unlicensed work |
| improper plumbing vents | code violations, likely unpermitted |
| additions with different ceiling heights | added without proper planning |
| mismatched floor levels | structural modification without engineering |
| no inspection stickers | work was never inspected |

### common unpermitted modifications

| modification | permit typically required? |
|--------------|---------------------------|
| garage conversion to living space | YES |
| basement/attic finish | YES |
| bathroom or kitchen addition | YES |
| electrical panel upgrade | YES |
| water heater replacement | YES (often skipped) |
| room addition or enclosed patio | YES |
| deck over certain height | YES |
| fence over certain height | varies by jurisdiction |

## .document comparison methods

### tax assessor records vs. reality

compare recorded square footage in county tax records against actual measurements.

| finding | indicates |
|---------|-----------|
| tax records show 1,500 sqft, house measures 1,800 sqft | ~300 sqft of unpermitted additions |
| extra bathroom not in records | unpermitted bathroom addition |
| "3 bedroom" in records but 4 bedrooms exist | unpermitted bedroom conversion |

### MLS history

review historical photos on Zillow, Redfin, or Realtor.com.

changes between listing periods without corresponding permits = red flag.

### aerial imagery

| source | what it shows |
|--------|---------------|
| Google Earth historical view | timestamped satellite photos of property over years |
| NETR Online | historical aerial imagery archives |
| county GIS | official parcel imagery with dates |

if structure appears between two dated images with no permit on file = unpermitted.

## .professional services

### permit research services

- some title companies offer permit research as add-on
- independent permit search companies exist in major metros
- cost: typically $100-300

### specialized inspectors

- request "permit verification" add-on to standard home inspection
- standard inspections explicitly EXCLUDE permit verification (per InterNACHI)
- must be specifically contracted

### enhanced title insurance

- ALTA Homeowner policies include Covered Risk 18
- covers costs when ordered to remove unpermitted structures
- subject to deductibles and limits
- ask title company about enhanced coverage options

## .red flags at purchase

| red flag | what to investigate |
|----------|---------------------|
| vague seller disclosure about modifications | request permit records |
| price significantly below comparables | may reflect known issues |
| garage doors replaced with walls/windows | garage conversion |
| bedrooms without egress windows | unpermitted bedroom conversion |
| bathrooms in unusual locations | converted closet or other space |
| recent work with no permit records | ask seller for documentation |
| seller hesitant to discuss improvements | potential undisclosed issues |

## .the detection checklist

### before offer

- [ ] search online permit portal for property address
- [ ] compare tax assessor square footage to listing
- [ ] review MLS photo history for changes
- [ ] check aerial imagery for structure additions

### at due diligence

- [ ] request complete permit history from building department
- [ ] add permit verification to home inspection scope
- [ ] compare permit scopes to actual work visible
- [ ] ask seller directly about all modifications

### if unpermitted work is found

- [ ] get estimates for retroactive permit costs
- [ ] negotiate price reduction or seller correction
- [ ] consider enhanced title insurance
- [ ] consult attorney about disclosure obligations if you proceed

---

## .key facts

| fact | implication |
|------|-------------|
| standard inspections exclude permits | you must specifically request permit verification |
| title insurance excludes unpermitted work | enhanced policies may help but have limits |
| liability transfers to buyer | you own the problem after close |
| "as-is" does not protect you | liability still transfers |
| sellers must disclose known issues | but may not know about prior owner work |

## .subtle unpermitted work (hardest to detect)

### the problem

some unpermitted work is invisible:
- no square footage change
- no exterior change
- no aerial imagery evidence
- passes standard inspection

but creates real liability when insurance claims are filed.

### common subtle unpermitted work

| work type | why it's invisible | how insurers find it |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| electrical panel upgrade | same location, no exterior change | permit search shows no electrical permit |
| rewired circuits | inside walls | material age vs. permit history |
| added outlets/fixtures | minor visible change | code violation patterns |
| water heater replacement | same location | permit search, contractor interviews |
| bathroom remodel (same footprint) | no size change | permit search, fixture age |
| HVAC replacement | same location | permit search, equipment serial/date |
| replaced plumbing | inside walls | material type vs. home age |
| interior wall removal | no exterior change | structural permit search |
| kitchen remodel | no size change | permit search, fixture age |

### detection methods for subtle work

#### 1. permit history vs. equipment age

| step | what to check |
|------|---------------|
| pull permit records | list all electrical, plumbing, HVAC permits |
| compare to equipment | panel manufacture date, water heater install date, HVAC serial |
| look for gaps | 2018 panel but no electrical permit since 1995 = unpermitted |

#### 2. material age analysis

| material | how to date it |
|----------|---------------|
| electrical panel | manufacturer label, model lookup, breaker styles |
| wire insulation | insulation type (cloth=old, romex colors indicate era) |
| pipes | pipe material (galvanized=pre-1970, PEX=post-2000) |
| water heater | serial number decodes to manufacture date |
| HVAC | serial number, refrigerant type (R-22=pre-2010) |

#### 3. code compliance patterns

work done without permits often violates code requirements that existed at install time:

| code requirement | era | if absent, suggests |
|------------------|-----|---------------------|
| AFCI breakers in bedrooms | 2002+ | post-2002 unpermitted panel work |
| GFCI in bathrooms | 1975+ | post-1975 unpermitted bathroom work |
| GFCI in kitchens | 1987+ | post-1987 unpermitted kitchen work |
| tamper-resistant outlets | 2008+ | post-2008 unpermitted outlet work |

#### 4. hire specialists (not standard inspectors)

| specialist | what they check |
|------------|-----------------|
| licensed electrician | panel age, wire methods, code compliance at install date |
| licensed plumber | pipe materials, vent compliance, fixture dates |
| HVAC technician | equipment age, refrigerant type, install quality |

**cost:** $100-300 per trade for inspection + permit research

### the uncomfortable math

| scenario | detection difficulty |
|----------|---------------------|
| room addition | EASY — visible in aerial, square footage change |
| garage conversion | EASY — visible exterior change |
| deck/patio | EASY — visible in aerial |
| electrical panel upgrade | HARD — no visible change |
| circuit rewire | HARD — inside walls |
| pipe replacement | HARD — inside walls |
| water heater | MEDIUM — visible but often overlooked |
| bathroom remodel | MEDIUM — fixtures visible but permits rarely checked |

### insurance exposure from subtle work

subtle unpermitted work sits quietly for years. then:

1. fire, flood, or failure occurs
2. you file insurance claim
3. adjuster searches permit database
4. finds no permit for the work involved
5. claim denied under code violation exclusion or material misrepresentation

**by then it's too late.** the prior owner is gone. the seller disclosure was vague. your inspection passed. title insurance won't help.

#### why insurers care about subtle work

| unpermitted work | insurance risk |
|------------------|----------------|
| electrical panel | fire cause, claim denied |
| rewired circuits | fire cause, claim denied |
| water heater | flood/fire cause, claim denied |
| plumbing pipes | leak/flood cause, claim denied |
| HVAC | fire/carbon monoxide cause, claim denied |

insurers investigate ANY claim involving electrical, plumbing, or HVAC systems. if the damaged system was unpermitted, expect denial.

#### the claim denial sequence

```
fire starts at electrical panel
    ↓
adjuster inspects, sees panel is newer than home
    ↓
adjuster searches permit database
    ↓
no electrical permit found
    ↓
insurer denies claim citing:
  - code violation exclusion, OR
  - faulty workmanship exclusion, OR
  - material misrepresentation (if you failed to disclose)
```

#### case law that applies

- **Sebo v. American Home (Fla. 2016)** — coverage MAY exist if covered peril also involved
- **Garvey v. State Farm (Cal. 1989)** — efficient proximate cause may save claim
- but these require litigation. most homeowners settle for denial or reduced payout.

### practical guidance

**minimum due diligence for subtle work:**

- [ ] pull complete permit history for property
- [ ] compare permit dates to visible equipment ages
- [ ] look for electrical permit if panel appears newer than home
- [ ] look for plumbing permit if water heater appears newer than home
- [ ] look for HVAC permit if system appears newer than home
- [ ] hire trade-specific inspectors if red flags appear

**questions to ask seller:**

- "when was the electrical panel last replaced?"
- "when was the water heater installed?"
- "have any electrical, plumbing, or HVAC repairs been done?"
- "do you have permits and inspection records for that work?"

**if seller is vague or defensive:** assume unpermitted work exists.
