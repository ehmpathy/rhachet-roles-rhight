# howto.shore-up-inherited-unpermitted-work.indianapolis-in

## .what

Indianapolis-specific guide to legitimize inherited unpermitted work via retroactive permits through DBNS (Department of Business and Neighborhood Services).

## .why

- Indianapolis DBNS "typically works with innocent purchasers"
- retroactive permits cost $150-700 per system (vs $50k+ denied claim)
- same-day or 1-2 day turnaround for simple permits
- homeowners can pull their own permits for primary residence

## penalty structure: inherited vs self-done work

### do penalties apply to all trades?

**yes** — the 2x retroactive penalty applies uniformly across electrical, plumbing, and HVAC in Indianapolis. the penalty is based on the standard permit fee, not the trade type.

| trade | standard fee range | retroactive penalty | penalty amount |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| electrical | $50-350 | 2x | $100-700 |
| plumbing | $50-300 | 2x | $100-600 |
| HVAC | $75-200 | 2x | $150-400 |

source: [Cobb County Fee Schedule](https://assets.cobbcounty.gov/files/2024-06/2024%20Fee%20Schedule%20Update_Final.pdf) (shows uniform 2x across trades), [Crest Real Estate](https://www.crestrealestate.com/how-much-does-a-retroactive-permit-cost/)

### inherited work vs work you did yourself

**key distinction**: jurisdictions often treat innocent purchasers differently from homeowners who did the work themselves.

| scenario | typical treatment |
|----------|-------------------|
| **you did the work without permit** | full 2x penalty applies |
| **previous owner did the work** | penalty waiver possible |
| **unknown who did work** | treated as inherited (lenient) |

> "If you are an innocent purchaser, the building department will likely work with you to resolve the issue, possibly offering deadline flexibility and waiving penalties."
> — [Kukun](https://mykukun.com/blog/how-to-legalize-unpermitted-renovations)

> "They most likely won't charge you for any penalties a retroactive permit incurs, and might give you more flexible deadlines to bring the work up to code if you are a subsequent owner who obtains permits for a previous owner's work."
> — [Permit Talk Forum](https://www.permittingtalk.com/threads/how-do-i-bring-unpermitted-work-up-to-code.5251/)

### how to trigger leniency (indianapolis)

when you call DBNS, say:

> "I purchased this property on [date]. I recently discovered [electrical/plumbing/HVAC] work that was done without permits — I believe by a previous owner. I'd like to obtain a retroactive permit and was told you may waive the penalty for inherited work."

**what helps your case**:

| evidence | why it helps |
|----------|--------------|
| purchase date | proves you didn't do the work |
| home inspection report | documents pre-purchase state |
| equipment age (manufacture date) | shows work predates your ownership |
| seller disclosure form | may show seller didn't disclose |
| permit search results | shows no permit on record |

### jurisdictions with no penalty

some jurisdictions charge standard fees only (no 2x multiplier) for retroactive permits:

| jurisdiction | policy |
|--------------|--------|
| Henderson, NV | no penalty fee |
| some CA jurisdictions | amnesty programs waive penalties |
| voluntary disclosure | often triggers reduced or waived penalty |

source: [Kukun](https://mykukun.com/blog/how-to-legalize-unpermitted-renovations), [Permit Talk](https://www.permittingtalk.com/threads/how-do-i-bring-unpermitted-work-up-to-code.5251/)

### worst case vs best case (indianapolis)

| scenario | penalty | total cost |
|----------|---------|------------|
| you did work, no disclosure | full 2x | $300-700 |
| you did work, voluntary disclosure | 2x (possibly reduced) | $200-500 |
| inherited work, no evidence | 2x or waived | $150-500 |
| inherited work, with evidence | likely waived | $100-350 (standard fee only) |

**recommendation**: gather evidence of purchase date and equipment age before you apply.

## who pulls the permit: homeowner vs contractor

### the difference

| who pulls | responsibility | liability | insurance |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| **contractor** | contractor ensures code compliance | contractor liable for defects | contractor's insurance covers |
| **homeowner** | homeowner ensures code compliance | homeowner liable for defects | homeowner's insurance may NOT cover |

> "If no permit is obtained, you (the owner) are legally responsible."
> — [Miami-Dade County: Contractor & Owner-Builder Responsibilities](https://wwwx.miamidade.gov/global/economy/building/contractor-owner-builder-responsibilities.page)

> "A permit makes the contractor legally responsible for their portion of the work."
> — [NYC Buildings: Project Requirements](https://www.nyc.gov/site/buildings/dob/project-requirements-contractor-permit-and-insurance.page)

### indianapolis rule

> "Any construction that requires a permit requires the applicant to be either a Marion County licensed contractor or the property owner."
> — Indianapolis DBNS

**homeowner CAN pull permits** for work on their primary residence. but for retroactive permits on inherited work, a licensed contractor should:
- inspect and certify the work meets code
- answer technical questions from inspector
- pull the permit on your behalf (recommended)

### why contractor-pulled is better for retroactive permits

| factor | homeowner pulls | contractor pulls |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| technical questions at inspection | you must answer | contractor answers |
| code compliance certification | you certify | contractor certifies |
| liability if work fails | falls on you | falls on contractor |
| insurance coverage | may be excluded | contractor insured |
| inspector confidence | lower | higher |

### red flag

> "The contractor who performed the work will be responsible for bringing the project into compliance at his or her expense, including any penalties imposed by the building department."
> — [Miami-Dade County](https://wwwx.miamidade.gov/global/economy/building/contractor-owner-builder-responsibilities.page)

> "Unpermitted work can void homeowner's insurance claims, delay property sales, and require costly demolition and reinspection."
> — [Indiana Contractor Authority](https://indianacontractorauthority.com/indiana-contractor-services-frequently-asked-questions)

### owner-builder insurance warning

if you pull your own permit as "owner-builder":

| risk | coverage |
|------|----------|
| standard homeowner's policy | may NOT cover construction accidents |
| liability for hired workers | you're responsible if they're uninsured |
| builder's risk / course of construction | does NOT include liability |

> "An owner-builder assumes responsibility for the overall job which may include, but is not limited to, taxes, insurance, and other legal liabilities."
> — [Ventura County Owner-Builder Handout](https://rma.venturacounty.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/considering-becoming-an-owner-builder-handout.pdf)

**recommendation for retroactive permits**: have the licensed contractor pull the permit and certify the work.

## permit timeline: before vs after work

### the rule

**permits are legally required BEFORE work begins.**

> "The property owner is responsible for ensuring that all applicable permits are applied for and obtained, prior to any construction beginning."
> — [American Fork, UT (gov)](https://www.americanfork.gov/Faq.aspx?QID=67)

> "Reminder: Building Permits Are Not Optional... A contractor may not obtain permits for construction work without first entering a contract to perform improvements."
> — [California CSLB (gov)](https://www.cslb.ca.gov/Newsletter/2018-summer/Building_Permits.asp)

> "Contractors working in trades that require a building permit for their project must 'pull' it at the building department before starting a job."
> — [Philadelphia L&I (gov)](https://www.phila.gov/departments/department-of-licenses-and-inspections/inspections/contractor-requirements/)

### timeline requirements

| milestone | typical rule |
|-----------|--------------|
| permit obtained | BEFORE work starts |
| work must start | within 180 days of permit |
| work cannot stop | for more than 180 days |
| extensions | request in writing, 180-day increments |

source: [Construction Concept](http://www.constructionconcept.net/permit-deadlines-and-expiration-rules-understanding-michigan-building-code/)

### consequences of no permit before work

| consequence | detail |
|-------------|--------|
| stop-work order | city can halt project |
| double/triple fees | 2x-3x standard permit cost |
| tear-down order | may require demolition |
| inspection difficulty | inspector can't see concealed work |
| insurance risk | claim denial if work causes damage |

### can you get a permit after work starts?

**yes** — but it's harder and more expensive:

> "There are a lot of hoops to jump through when getting permits for work that is already completed. It's often a much trickier process, takes longer, and costs more."
> — [Lamont Bros](https://lamontbros.com/permits/can-i-get-a-building-permit-after-the-work-is-done-yes-heres-how/)

### retroactive permit vs "work in progress" permit

| scenario | permit type | penalty |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| work not started yet | standard permit | none |
| work in progress (caught) | retroactive | 2x-3x |
| work complete (voluntary) | retroactive / as-built | 2x (may be waived) |
| work complete (inherited) | retroactive / as-built | often waived |

**key point**: there's no grace period. permit must be pulled BEFORE work starts. anything after = retroactive with penalties (unless inherited).

sources:
- [California CSLB: Building Permits Are Not Optional (gov)](https://www.cslb.ca.gov/Newsletter/2018-summer/Building_Permits.asp)
- [Philadelphia L&I: Contractor Requirements (gov)](https://www.phila.gov/departments/department-of-licenses-and-inspections/inspections/contractor-requirements/)
- [American Fork, UT: Permit FAQ (gov)](https://www.americanfork.gov/Faq.aspx?QID=67)

## the process (indianapolis-specific)

### step 1: call DBNS first

before you apply, call (317) 327-8700 and explain:

> "I purchased this property in [year]. I discovered unpermitted [electrical/plumbing/HVAC] work that appears to have been done by a previous owner. I'd like to pursue a retroactive permit."

**what to expect**:
- DBNS staff will explain the "as-built" permit process
- they may waive or reduce the 2x penalty for innocent purchasers
- they'll clarify which documents you need

> "Indianapolis DBNS typically works with innocent purchasers. Explain your situation when you apply — they may waive or reduce penalties."
> — ref.indianapolis-electrical-retroactive-permit

### step 2: hire licensed contractor

for retroactive permits, hire a Marion County licensed contractor to:

| task | purpose |
|------|---------|
| inspect current work | assess if it meets code |
| certify compliance | provide written affidavit |
| identify deficiencies | list what needs correction |
| pull the permit | submit "as-built" application |

**indianapolis electrician levels**:

| level | can pull permit? | can certify work? |
|-------|------------------|-------------------|
| apprentice | NO | NO |
| journeyman | limited | NO |
| master electrician | YES | YES |

> "Any contractor must be or employ a master electrician."
> — ref.electrical-license-structure.indiana

**find licensed contractors**: [Indy.gov Licensed Contractors](https://www.indy.gov/activity/licensed-contractors)

### step 3: gather documentation

| document | why needed |
|----------|------------|
| proof of purchase | shows when you acquired property |
| photos of work | shows current state |
| home inspection report | may document visible upgrades |
| contractor assessment | certifies code compliance |
| load calculation (electrical) | required for panel work per NEC 220 |

### step 4: apply for retroactive permit

submit via [Accela portal](https://aca-prod.accela.com/indy/Default.aspx) or in person:

| field | what to enter |
|-------|---------------|
| permit type | select trade (electrical, mechanical, plumbing) |
| work description | mark as "as-built" or "retroactive" |
| contractor | licensed contractor info |
| notes | "work done by previous owner prior to my purchase on [date]" |

### step 5: inspection

inspector examines:

| item | what they check |
|------|-----------------|
| installation quality | proper methods |
| code compliance | NEC/UPC/IMC standards |
| safety | no hazards |
| labels | breakers, shutoffs marked |
| access | required clearances |

**timeline**: 1-2 weeks after permit approval

### step 6: corrections (if needed)

| scenario | action |
|----------|--------|
| minor deficiency | contractor fixes, schedule re-inspection |
| major deficiency | scope work, fix, re-inspect |
| re-inspection fee | $50-100 each |

### step 7: final approval

once passed:
- permit status updated to "finalized" in Accela
- property record shows permitted work
- obtain printout for insurance documentation

## homeowner exemption (indianapolis)

Indianapolis allows homeowners to pull their own permits:

> "Any construction that requires a permit requires the applicant to be either a Marion County licensed contractor or the property owner."
> — Indianapolis DBNS

| situation | can pull permit? |
|-----------|------------------|
| work on your primary residence | YES |
| work on rental property you own | NO — need contractor |
| work for someone else | NO — need contractor |

**however, for retroactive permits**:

even if homeowner exemption applies, you still need a licensed contractor to **certify** that the previous owner's work meets code. the homeowner exemption lets you pull the permit, but the inspection still requires professional validation for work you didn't personally perform.

## indiana insurance context

### the sheehan construction case

**Sheehan Construction Co. v. Continental Casualty Co. (Ind. 2010)** — Indiana Supreme Court:

| rule | implication |
|------|-------------|
| faulty workmanship MAY constitute an "occurrence" | coverage possible for defective work |
| subcontractor exception applies | if sub did work, coverage more likely |
| unintentional defects can trigger coverage | negligence ≠ automatic denial |

**key distinction**:

| who did unpermitted work? | coverage outlook |
|---------------------------|------------------|
| subcontractor | coverage likely (subcontractor exception) |
| homeowner/GC directly | denial more likely ("your work" exclusion) |
| previous owner (unknown) | analyze as subcontractor scenario |

source: [PSRB Indiana Insurance Law](https://www.psrb.com/2024/05/navigating-homeowners-insurance-claims-in-indiana/)

### forum anecdotes

> "Insurance companies will pay any claim for a covered peril regardless of the cause... every homeowner's policy I've seen covers the insured against fire from electrical causes."
> — [DoItYourself Forum](https://www.doityourself.com/forum/electrical-ac-dc/410442-can-insurance-company-deny-claim-if-unpermitted-work-causes-fire.html)

> "I have observed insurance companies pay out on fires caused by very poor electrical work that never met code."
> — [Electrician Talk Forum](https://www.electriciantalk.com/threads/non-permitted-insurance-claims.36989/)

**but also**:

> "If your insurance company discovers unpermitted work on your property, they may raise your monthly insurance premium or cancel your policy altogether."
> — [Nicholas Design Build](https://nicholasdesignbuild.com/penalties-for-building-without-a-permit/)

### bottom line for indiana

denials are rare but documented. the safest path: retroactive permit eliminates the risk entirely for $150-700 per system.
