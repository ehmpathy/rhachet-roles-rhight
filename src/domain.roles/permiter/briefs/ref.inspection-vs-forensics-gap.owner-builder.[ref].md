# ref.inspection-vs-forensics-gap.owner-builder

## .what

when a homeowner performs electrical work WITH permit and inspection, fire forensics can still identify workmanship defects that passed inspection. this brief examines the exposure AFTER permit approval — not unpermitted work.

## .why

- permit approval does not guarantee defect-free work
- forensics can find defects invisible at inspection
- owner-builders have no subrogation target for recovery
- case law clarifies when coverage applies despite defects

## case law: when permitted work with defects is COVERED

### Fruchthandler v. Tri-State Consumer Insurance Co. (NY 2019)

**citation**: 2019 NY Slip Op 02502 (App. Div. 2d Dept. 2019)

**facts**: owners of two-family home had fire. insurer disclaimed coverage based on faulty workmanship exclusion, claiming "improper conditions" in junction box "were the direct cause of the fire."

**ruling**: court granted summary judgment TO THE HOMEOWNERS. ensuing loss exception applied.

**reasoning**: 
- fire occurred TWO YEARS after alleged faulty workmanship
- fire caused "ensuing loss to property wholly separate from the defective property itself"
- faulty junction box ≠ entire house burned

**key quote**: the exception to the faulty workmanship exclusion preserves coverage when fire constitutes ensuing loss separate from defective property.

**relevance**: establishes that workmanship defect → fire → coverage applies under ensuing loss doctrine.

**source**: [NY Appellate Digest](https://www.newyorkappellatedigest.com/2019/04/03/the-exception-to-the-faulty-workmanship-exclusion-in-the-fire-insurance-policy-applied-to-preserve-coverage-for-ensuing-loss-second-dept/)

### Gardens Condominium v. Farmers Insurance (WA 2024)

**citation**: 2024 WL (Wash. 2024)

**facts**: faulty roof construction caused inadequate ventilation → condensation → water damage.

**ruling**: Washington Supreme Court ruled coverage exists. ensuing loss exception applies.

**reasoning**: faulty workmanship caused condensation (covered peril), which caused damage. ensuing loss preserved coverage.

**source**: [Justia](https://law.justia.com/cases/washington/supreme-court/2024/101-892-4.html)

## case law: when workmanship defects are NOT covered

### Bob Robison v. RLI Insurance (8th Cir. 2025)

**citation**: 2025 WL (8th Cir. 2025)

**facts**: contractor's faulty painting required floor replacement. insured claimed ensuing loss.

**ruling**: coverage DENIED.

**reasoning**: 
- no separate covered peril identified
- faulty workmanship was "sole and exclusive cause"
- damage occurred "the moment paint was applied"
- ensuing loss requires: defect → SEPARATE covered peril → damage

**key distinction**: no intervening covered peril (like fire). direct defect damage.

**source**: [Cozen O'Connor](https://www.propertyinsurancelawobserver.com/2025/04/01/coverage-for-defective-workmanship-not-restored-by-ensuing-loss-provision/)

### Rocky Mountain Prestress v. Liberty Mutual (10th Cir. 2020)

**ruling**: when faulty workmanship is the sole cause, exclusion applies. ensuing loss exception requires a second, covered cause.

**source**: [National Law Review](https://natlawreview.com/article/resulting-loss-exception-does-not-restore-coverage-faulty-workmanship)

## the legal framework: ensuing loss

### how it works

| scenario | coverage? |
|----------|-----------|
| defect → fire → damage | YES (fire is covered peril) |
| defect → water intrusion → damage | YES (water is covered peril) |
| defect → more defect damage | NO (no separate covered peril) |
| defect → collapse → damage | YES (collapse is covered peril) |

### the key test

> "There have to be two causes of a loss, one excluded and one not excluded. One example is where acts taken during construction cause a fire—the fire is a covered peril and therefore there is coverage."

**source**: [IRMI - Ensuing Loss](https://www.irmi.com/articles/expert-commentary/ensuing-loss-getting-around-a-property-policys-defective-construction-exclusion)

### applied to owner-builder electrical work

| sequence | analysis | coverage |
|----------|----------|----------|
| loose connection → arc → fire | fire is separate covered peril | YES |
| undersized wire → overheat → fire | fire is separate covered peril | YES |
| improper splice → arc fault → fire | fire is separate covered peril | YES |
| defect → defect worsens (no fire) | no separate covered peril | NO |

**conclusion**: electrical defects that cause fires are typically COVERED under ensuing loss.

## the owner-builder difference: recovery

### the subrogation gap

**with contractor**:
| event | outcome |
|-------|---------|
| fire from defect | insurer pays claim |
| forensics proves contractor defect | subrogation |
| contractor's insurance pays insurer | recovery |
| net | everyone whole |

**with owner-builder**:
| event | outcome |
|-------|---------|
| fire from defect | insurer pays claim (likely) |
| forensics proves YOUR defect | no subrogation target |
| insurer absorbs loss | no recovery |
| net | you're covered, insurer loses |

### why insurers still pay

| factor | effect |
|--------|--------|
| ensuing loss doctrine | fire is covered peril |
| Fruchthandler precedent | courts favor coverage |
| litigation risk | denial may fail in court |
| subrogation futile | can't sue homeowner for their own claim |

## practical exposure analysis

### what permit + inspection provides

| protection | mechanism |
|------------|-----------|
| evidence of compliance | work passed examination |
| burden shift | insurer must prove defect |
| Fruchthandler defense | ensuing loss preserves coverage |
| no subrogation pursuit | insurer can't recover from you |

### what remains at risk

| risk | likelihood | mitigation |
|------|------------|------------|
| denial attempt | LOW (case law favors coverage) | document everything |
| bad faith denial | VERY LOW (precedent clear) | attorney if needed |

## the honest answer

| question | answer |
|----------|--------|
| can permitted owner-builder work be denied? | theoretically, if no ensuing loss |
| has it happened? | no documented cases found |
| why not? | fire IS the ensuing loss → coverage |
| what's the real risk? | no subrogation recovery |

**bottom line**: 
- fire from permitted work is likely covered under ensuing loss
- the difference from contractor is RECOVERY, not DENIAL
- insurer pays you either way
- with contractor: insurer recovers via subrogation
- with owner-builder: insurer absorbs the loss
