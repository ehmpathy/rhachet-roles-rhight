## documented cases

### case 1: fire in renovation — extant structure denied

From [US Assure](https://usassure.com/resources/articles/builders-risk-insurance-lessons-learned-from-claim-setbacks):
> "coverage was only triggered for 80% of the $200,000 renovation work and the $400,000 extant structure coverage was denied"

**what happened:**
- homeowner had renovation in progress
- fire occurred
- wrong policy type selected at application

**denial reason:** extant structure coverage not selected

**lesson:** for renovations, must explicitly select "extant structure" coverage — renovation-only policies exclude the pre-renovation structure value.

---

### case 2: fire after tenant occupancy — coverage ended

From [US Assure](https://usassure.com/resources/articles/7-tips-to-avoid-common-residential-builders-risk-claim-problems):
> "coverage ends 60 days after the property is occupied in whole or in part, or put to its intended use"

**what happened:**
- renovation complete
- tenant moved in
- fire occurred after occupancy

**denial reason:** occupancy triggered coverage termination

**lesson:** builder's risk ends when property is occupied or used. must transition to standard HO/DP policy before occupancy.

---

### case 3: pipe burst in vacancy — day 61

From insurance industry sources:
> "pipe burst on day 61 could result in entire claim denial"

**what happened:**
- property vacant in renovation pause
- vacancy exceeded 60-day threshold
- water damage from burst pipe

**denial reason:** vacancy clause suspended water damage coverage

**typical vacancy clause exclusions:**
- vandalism
- water damage
- glass breakage
- sprinkler leakage

**lesson:** vacancy clauses are 30-60 days per carrier. maintain activity at site or notify carrier of extended vacancy.

---

### case 4: theft in construction — missed reports

From [US Assure](https://usassure.com/resources/articles/builders-risk-insurance-lessons-learned-from-claim-setbacks):
> "Because just 66.7% of the inventory reports were submitted (four out of six), that percentage of the loss could be paid and then the deductible applied"

**what happened:**
- materials stolen in construction
- $11,230 claim filed
- only 4 of 6 required monthly reports submitted

**payout:** $4,990 instead of full claim

**lesson:** submit ALL required reports on schedule. missed reports reduce payout proportionally.

---

### case 5: hurricane — pre-inception work excluded

From [US Assure](https://usassure.com/resources/articles/builders-risk-insurance-lessons-learned-from-claim-setbacks):
> "Because the policy has an exclusion for structures that are over 30% complete (unless UW approval is granted), any work performed prior to the policy's inception date will not be covered"

**what happened:**
- construction 75% complete before policy bound
- hurricane caused damage

**payout:** only $15,000 covered

**lesson:** bind policy BEFORE construction starts. if already underway, get UW approval in text.

## denial patterns for residential

| pattern | frequency | severity |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| **admin errors** | common | partial or full denial |
| **occupancy triggers** | common | full denial |
| **vacancy clause** | moderate | exclusion of specific perils |
| **pre-inception work** | moderate | partial denial |
| **wrong coverage type** | moderate | full denial of uncovered portion |

---

## residential-specific factors

| factor | residential risk |
|--------|------------------|
| **owner-occupier temptation** | higher risk of early occupancy |
| **diy work** | permit and inspection compliance gaps |
| **vacancy** | renovation pauses common |
| **report compliance** | less likely to have dedicated staff |
| **policy comprehension** | homeowners less familiar than contractors |

## what court cases show

searched for Zurich and carrier-specific residential claim denial court cases:
- most residential claims settle before litigation
- court cases typically involve larger commercial projects
- residential homeowner cases rarely reach published opinions

**implication:** residential denials are administrative, not litigated. carriers apply policy language strictly; homeowners don't have resources to fight.
