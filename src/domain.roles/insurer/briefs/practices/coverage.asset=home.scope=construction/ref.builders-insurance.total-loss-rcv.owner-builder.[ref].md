# ref.insurance-home-total-loss-replacement-cost-value.owner-builder

## .what

when an owner-builder's work causes total loss of their home, builder's risk policies often pay only **depreciated value (ACV)** for the prior structure — not full replacement cost (RCV).

## .why this matters

- $3k DIY electrical job causes fire → $400k house destroyed
- you expect $400k payout to rebuild
- policy pays **depreciated value** of prior structure
- 20-year-old house might pay out $320k or less
- you're $80k+ short of rebuild cost
- **unpermitted work can result in complete denial**

---

## the gap

| component | typical valuation |
|-----------|-------------------|
| new work (the $3k electrical) | RCV (full cost) |
| prior structure (the $400k house) | **ACV (depreciated)** |

> "For renovation policies, the existing structure is typically insured at a depreciated value (actual cash value) while all renovations are insured as full replacement cost."
> — [Builder's Shield](https://www.buildersshield.com/28255/replacementcostvsactualcashvalue/)

> "Many builders risk policies provide only actual cash value coverage for existing structures... which could be a 30-50 percent shortfall."
> — [IRMI](https://www.irmi.com/articles/expert-commentary/key-considerations-when-buying-builders-risk-coverage)

---

## owner-builder specific risks

### unpermitted work = denial

| scenario | outcome |
|----------|---------|
| permitted DIY work + fire | **COVERED** — ensuing loss exception |
| unpermitted DIY work + fire | **may be denied entirely** |
| permitted DIY work + defect only (no fire) | NOT covered — no covered peril |

insurers routinely search permit databases after fires.

### key result: no documented denials for PERMITTED owner-builder fire claims

after exhaustive search of case law and forums: **no cases found** where owner-builder pulled permit, passed inspection, had fire, and was denied.

> "The exception to the faulty workmanship exclusion in the fire insurance policy applied to preserve coverage for ensuing loss."
> — *Fruchthandler v. Tri-State Consumer Insurance* (NY 2019), [NY Appellate Digest](https://www.newyorkappellatedigest.com/2019/04/03/the-exception-to-the-faulty-workmanship-exclusion-in-the-fire-insurance-policy-applied-to-preserve-coverage-for-ensuing-loss-second-dept/)

**all documented denials involve**:
- unpermitted work (Bridgewater, Lakewood cases)
- direct defect damage without fire (*Bob Robison v. RLI*, 8th Cir. 2025)
- policy condition violations (occupancy, late notice, misrepresentation)

### case study: bridgewater, NJ — unpermitted basement

- electrical fire burned down entire house
- fire started in finished basement
- **permit status**: no permits for basement work
- **insurance outcome**: claim DENIED after permit database search
- source: [Houzz Forum](https://www.houzz.com/discussions/2585251/no-final-permits-no-insurance-pay-out)

### case study: lakewood, CO — DIY light fixture

- homeowner replaced light fixture without proper wire knowledge
- fire started in ceiling 6 months later
- **damage claimed**: $175,000
- **insurance outcome**: claim DENIED — insurer determined DIY installation caused fire
- **DIY savings**: ~$150 (by not hiring professional)
- quote: "That decision ultimately cost them their life savings"
- source: [ElectricAll](https://electricall.co/why-diy-electrical-work-can-void-your-home-insurance-what-homeowners-need-to-know/)

### case study: santa clarita, CA — "the broke family"

- couple purchased home with room addition disclosed as "built to code but not permitted"
- 4 years later: structural problems (doors frozen, ceiling cracks, roof tiles that fell)
- investigation revealed: unpermitted electrical, modified trusses, tampered plumbing/gas lines
- **repairs needed**: ~$100,000
- **insurance outcome**: claim DENIED due to "construction defect"
- **total debt after 4-year legal battle**: ~$500,000 (mortgage + refinance + legal fees)
- **outcome**: "eliminating retirement prospects"
- source: [DoItYourself Forum](https://www.doityourself.com/forum/electrical-ac-dc/410442-can-insurance-company-deny-claim-if-unpermitted-work-causes-fire.html)

## when DIY fires ARE covered

not all owner-builder fires result in denial. key factors:

| factor | favors coverage | disfavors coverage |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| permits | pulled and passed | none or failed |
| cause | covered peril (fire) | faulty workmanship itself |
| documentation | photos, receipts | none |
| policy language | ensuing loss clause | strict exclusions |

### case study: connecticut claims adjuster testimony

insurance adjuster shared cases that WERE paid:
- homeowner in attic soldering pipe caught insulation on fire, burned house down — **PAID**
- homeowner installed outlet backwards, caused fire — **PAID**
- quote: "Insurance policies cover stupidity"
- source: [Fine Homebuilding Forum](https://www.finehomebuilding.com/forum/homeowners-insurance-unlicensed-unins)

### case study: mike holt forum — claims adjuster daughter

- forum member's daughter works as claims adjuster
- quote: "They pay out tons of money for claims involving poor workmanship"
- another user (7 years of inspected damage claims): "Never participated in a case where insurance refused to pay because of unpermitted work"
- **caveat**: insurers may subrogate against whoever performed work after payment
- source: [Mike Holt Forums](https://forums.mikeholt.com/threads/does-ho-insurance-cover-damage-due-to-unpermitted-work.3575/)

## the ensuing loss exception

even when faulty workmanship is excluded, **fire damage from that work is often covered**:

| what | covered? |
|------|----------|
| faulty wire work itself | NO |
| fire caused by faulty wire work | YES (ensuing loss) |
| structure damage from fire | YES |
| cost to redo wire work | NO |

> "If there's faulty electrical equipment at a construction site that causes a fire—this would exclude the cost of replace and install that faulty component, but the rest of the loss is covered."
> — [MEREDA](https://mereda.org/2021/09/14/the-faulty-workmanship-exclusion-in-builders-risk-insurance-measure-thrice-cut-once/)

- source: [United Policyholders](https://uphelp.org/claim-guidance-publications/underinsurance-101/)

## owner-builder protection checklist

| action | why |
|--------|-----|
| pull permits before work | avoids denial for unpermitted work |
| ask: "is prior structure at RCV or ACV?" | determines payout on total loss |
| verify policy limit = full rebuild cost | avoids coinsurance penalty |
| document all work with photos | supports claim if needed |
| keep receipts for materials | proves value |
| check if homeowner's policy suspended | may have no coverage while construction active |

## case law: RCV vs ACV

### california fair plan v. garnes (2017)

- citation: 11 Cal.App.5th 1276
- court ruled "total loss" = physical destruction, not economic
- for partial losses: insured entitled to **repair cost minus depreciation**
- **key**: even if repair cost exceeds market value, ACV applies
- sources: [FindLaw](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/ca-court-of-appeal/1862181.html), [Justia](https://law.justia.com/cases/california/court-of-appeal/2017/a143190.html)

### jurisdictional variation in ACV

| state | ACV calculation |
|-------|-----------------|
| california | RCV minus depreciation of **materials only** (not labor) |
| new york | "broad evidence rule" — may consider market value |
| other states | varies |

source: [J.S. Held](https://www.jsheld.com/insights/articles/builders-risk-complexities-in-insuring-existing-property)

---

## bottom line

owner-builder total loss is **higher risk** than hired-builder:
- unpermitted work can trigger complete denial
- DIY quality may be scrutinized more
- no contractor liability to subrogate against

**pull permits. ask for RCV on prior structure. document all work.**
