# ref.text-messages-as-evidence

## .what

text messages (SMS, iMessage, WhatsApp) are admissible as evidence in court when properly authenticated.

## .why

layer 3 of the three-layer defense relies on written confirmation via text. this reference documents the legal basis for text messages as evidence.

---

## legal basis

### Federal Rules of Evidence Rule 901

Rule 901 requires that evidence be authenticated before admission. the standard is "prima facie" — the proponent must show enough that a reasonable finder of fact could conclude the evidence is genuine.

text messages can be authenticated through:
- testimony of a witness with knowledge
- the sender's known phone number
- distinctive characteristics (style, references to specific facts)
- circumstantial evidence (only the sender would know this information)
- metadata from the original message

> "the authentication standard applies equally to digital and traditional evidence, and the burden for authentication under Rule 901 is quite low"
> — American Bar Association

### admissibility requirements

| requirement | what it means |
|-------------|---------------|
| **relevance** | message relates to the matter at hand |
| **authentication** | proof of who sent it and that it's genuine |
| **legal acquisition** | obtained legally (not hacked or stolen) |
| **hearsay** | must overcome hearsay objections or fit an exception |

## what courts accept

| format | admissible? | strength |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| original message on device | yes | strongest — contains metadata |
| screenshot with timestamp and sender | yes | acceptable — can be verified against original |
| screenshot without context | weak | may be challenged as incomplete |
| verbal description of message | no | not written evidence |

---

## critical metadata

courts prefer original messages because metadata is harder to falsify:

| metadata | what it proves |
|----------|----------------|
| timestamp | when message was sent/received |
| sender phone number or ID | ties message to specific person |
| delivered/read receipts | message was actually received |
| device identifier | which device sent it |

> "screenshots can be easily edited or misrepresented. courts generally prefer the original messages complete with all metadata, which is much harder to falsify."
> — LeapXpert

---

## platform support

all major platforms are accepted:

| platform | admissible? |
|----------|-------------|
| SMS | yes |
| iMessage | yes |
| WhatsApp | yes |
| Facebook Messenger | yes |
| other message apps | yes, if authenticated |

> "text messages and other electronic communications are admissible as evidence in courts, inclusive of SMS, iMessage, WhatsApp messages, and voicemails"
> — CPM Legal

## how to preserve text evidence

### minimum (screenshot)
- capture full thread with your question and their answer
- timestamp must be visible
- sender contact name/number must be visible

### better (keep original)
- do not delete the conversation
- original contains metadata screenshots lose
- can be forensically extracted if needed

### best (export with metadata)
- iCloud or iTunes backup preserves full metadata
- forensic tools (Decipher TextMessage, Cellebrite) can export
- this is what attorneys use for formal proceedings

---

## for insurance claims

you are not headed to court — you are buildng evidence in case insurance denies a claim.

**minimum requirements:**
- screenshot of thread (your question + their confirmation)
- sender phone number visible
- keep original thread on device

this is sufficient to prove the contractor made the statement. if insurance disputes, the screenshot plus original on your phone provides authentication.
