# ref.subcontractor-coverage-case-law

## .what

court cases and policy endorsements that determine whether insurance covers damage caused by subcontractors.

## .why

- insurance coverage for subcontractor work varies by state and policy
- some policies strip out subcontractor coverage via endorsement CG 22 94
- if insurance denies AND contractor is insolvent, you may have no recovery path
- verification of coverage gaps is critical before work begins

---

## the subcontractor exception

standard CGL policies exclude damage to "your work" — but include an exception: the exclusion does not apply if the work was performed by a subcontractor.

this exception is critical. without it, all subcontractor damage is excluded.

---

## endorsement CG 22 94: the coverage killer

insurers can remove the subcontractor exception via endorsement CG 22 94 ("Exclusion — Damage to Work Performed by Subcontractors on Your Behalf").

| policy status | subcontractor damage covered? |
|---------------|-------------------------------|
| standard CGL | yes — subcontractor exception applies |
| CGL + CG 22 94 | **no** — exception stripped out |

insurers have been adding CG 22 94 to policies to reduce costs. the contractor may not know it's there.

### how to check

when you call to verify the COI, ask:

> "does this policy contain endorsement CG 22 94, which excludes coverage for work performed by subcontractors?"

if yes, and they send a subcontractor, you have a coverage gap.

## case law: where homeowners won

### Cypress Point v. Adria Towers (New Jersey Supreme Court, 2016)

**facts:** condo association sued developer over water damage from subcontractor's faulty roof and window work.

**insurer's argument:** "your work" exclusion applies — no coverage.

**court ruling:** subcontractor exception applies. the exclusion "did not apply if the damage or work out of which the damage arose was performed by a subcontractor."

**outcome:** insurance had to pay.

**citation:** [Cypress Point Condominium Association v. Adria Towers, 226 N.J. 403 (2016)](https://law.justia.com/cases/new-jersey/supreme-court/2016/a-1314-15.html)

---

### Acuity v. M/I Homes (Illinois Supreme Court, 2023)

**facts:** townhome owners sued over water damage from subcontractor construction defects.

**insurer's argument:** construction defects are not an "occurrence" — no coverage.

**court ruling:** overturned decades of Illinois precedent. water damage from subcontractor defects IS "property damage" caused by an "occurrence."

**outcome:** coverage applies; insurer must defend.

**significance:** described as "big win for contractors" and homeowner associations.

**citation:** [Acuity v. M/I Homes of Chicago, 2023 IL 129087](https://www.traublieberman.com/perspectives/illinois-supreme-court-holds-that-constructions-defects-may-constitute-property-damage-caused-by-an-occurrence-under-standard-cgl-policy-overruling-prior-appellate-court-precedent)

---

## case law: where insurance won

### Admiral Insurance v. Tocci Building (First Circuit, 2024)

**facts:** general contractor sued for damage caused by subcontractors — roof leaks, mold, soil settlement.

**insurer's argument:** all damage falls under "your work" exclusion because Tocci was general contractor for entire project.

**court ruling:** entire project was "your work" — subcontractor exception did NOT apply.

**outcome:** no duty to defend or pay.

**significance:** demonstrates that general contractors may lose subcontractor exception if they're responsible for the whole project.

**citation:** [Admiral Insurance Co. v. Tocci Building Corp., 120 F.4th 933 (1st Cir. 2024)](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca1/22-1462/22-1462-2024-11-08.html)

## state-by-state landscape

| jurisdiction | subcontractor exception status |
|--------------|-------------------------------|
| New Jersey | **applies** — Cypress Point (2016) |
| Illinois | **applies** — Acuity v. M/I Homes (2023) |
| Massachusetts | **limited** — Admiral v. Tocci (2024) |
| Colorado | statute provides some predictability |
| other states | varies — check local precedent |

**majority position:** since 2012, state supreme courts have reached "near unanimity" that subcontractor defects can be covered.

**minority position:** some states still hold construction defects cannot constitute an "occurrence."

## worst-case scenario: no recovery

| condition | result |
|-----------|--------|
| insurance denies (CG 22 94 or adverse state) | must sue contractor directly |
| contractor is solvent | recover from contractor's assets |
| contractor is bankrupt/insolvent | **may have no recovery path** |

### recovery options when contractor is insolvent

1. **contractor's insurance survives bankruptcy** — policy doesn't disappear with the company; you can still claim
2. **subcontractor's own insurance** — if the sub has coverage (but you're likely not additional insured)
3. **sue subcontractor directly** — difficult without direct contract
4. **your homeowner's insurance** — may cover damage, then subrogate

> "Going out of business or filing for bankruptcy does not negate an insurance policy."
> — Steinberg Law Firm

---

## verification checklist

before work begins, verify with the insurance company:

- [ ] policy is active
- [ ] you are listed as additional insured
- [ ] policy does NOT contain endorsement CG 22 94
- [ ] subcontractor work is covered under the policy
- [ ] if subcontractors will be used, their insurance is also verified
