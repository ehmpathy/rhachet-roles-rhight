# risk.subcontractor-gap

## .what

how insurance can deny a claim when a subcontractor performs the work, even after the contractor said they would do it themselves.

## .why

- subcontractors are not employees — they're separate businesses
- the general contractor's insurance covers **their** employees, not other businesses
- this is a common gap that voids your three-layer defense

---

## the scenario

1. contractor says "i'll do this job myself" (owner/employee)
2. you verify insurance, get COI, confirm additional insured
3. someone else shows up to do the work
4. that person is a subcontractor, not an employee
5. subcontractor causes damage
6. you file a claim
7. **insurance denies**

---

## why insurance denies

| your assumption | reality |
|-----------------|---------|
| "the business did the work" | a subcontractor did the work |
| "the policy covers the work" | policy covers employees, not subcontractors |
| "i verified all of it" | you verified the wrong party |

### the insurance company's argument

> "our policy covers ABC Electric LLC and its employees. the work was performed by XYZ Electrical Services, a separate business as a subcontractor. XYZ is not an employee of ABC, and their work is not covered under ABC's policy. the claim is denied."

---

## how the three layers fail

| layer | why it fails |
|-------|--------------|
| layer 1: insurance verification | you verified ABC's policy, but XYZ did the work |
| layer 2: permit | permit is under ABC's name, but ABC didn't do the work |
| layer 3: written confirmation | contractor said "i'll do it" but sent someone else |

the permit creates a complex situation:
- ABC is liable because the permit is in their name
- but ABC's insurance won't pay because ABC didn't do the work
- you'd have to sue ABC, then ABC sues XYZ
- or you sue XYZ directly — but you have no relationship with XYZ

---

## what you're left with

| path | outcome |
|------|---------|
| claim against ABC's insurance | denied — subcontractor not covered |
| sue ABC directly | ABC is liable (permit), but recovery is harder than insurance |
| claim against XYZ's insurance | you're not on their policy |
| sue XYZ directly | you have no contract with XYZ |

---

## legal precedent: contractor remains liable

even when insurance denies, **the contractor is still legally liable** under the nondelegable duty doctrine.

### key case law

**Dow v. Holly Manufacturing Co.** (California Supreme Court, 1958)

the court held that a general contractor who builds a house using subcontractors is treated the same as a manufacturer who installs parts from suppliers:

> "there is no valid distinction between a general contractor constructing a building and using subcontractors to install equipment therein, and the manufacturer of an automobile buying parts not fabricated by it to install in the car."

the contractor was liable for a defective heater installed by a subcontractor that caused deaths — the fact that they didn't discover the defect did not relieve them of liability.

**Crisostomo Vargas v. Inland Washington, LLC** (Washington Supreme Court, 2019)

the court established that:
- "a general contractor may not delegate its statutory duty"
- "if a general contractor delegates its own duties to a subcontractor, the general contractor will be liable for the subcontractor's breach"
- control test: "the right to exercise such control, and general supervisory functions being sufficient to establish control"

### what this means for you

| legal principle | implication |
|-----------------|-------------|
| nondelegable duty | contractor cannot escape liability by using subcontractors |
| permit liability | permit in their name = they're responsible for the work |
| misrepresentation | "i'll do it myself" + sent subcontractor = breach + leverage |

### the insurance coverage gap

even though the contractor is liable, their CGL (commercial general liability) policy may exclude subcontractor work:

| policy issue | effect |
|--------------|--------|
| "your work" exclusion | excludes damage caused by insured's own work or subcontractor work |
| subcontractor exception | some policies cover it, some don't — varies by carrier |
| Admiral Insurance v. Tocci Building (2024) | First Circuit found no duty to defend claims from defective subcontractor work |

### your recovery path

1. **insurance claim** — file against contractor's policy (may be denied)
2. **if denied, sue contractor directly** — nondelegable duty doctrine makes them liable
3. **your evidence**:
   - permit proves they're on the hook
   - text proves misrepresentation ("i'll do it" but sent subcontractor)
   - COI proves they have assets/insurance to recover from

## how to prevent this

### before work begins

if they said owner/employee would do the work, confirm when someone arrives:

> "are you an employee of [business name], or a subcontractor?"

### if a subcontractor shows up

**option 1: refuse the subcontractor**

> "i was told the owner would do this work. i'm not comfortable with a subcontractor unless i can verify their insurance."

**option 2: verify the subcontractor**

get from the subcontractor:
- their business name
- their license number
- their insurance certificate
- add yourself as additional insured on their policy too

**option 3: get written confirmation of coverage**

ask the general contractor:

> "you sent [subcontractor name] instead. can you confirm in text that your policy covers subcontractor work, or provide their insurance info?"

---

## red flags

| red flag | risk |
|----------|------|
| "my guy will come do it" | who is "my guy"? employee or sub? |
| different company name on the truck | likely a subcontractor |
| worker doesn't know the business name | subcontractor, not employee |
| "i'm just here to do the electrical" | may be sub who doesn't know the arrangement |
| contractor gets defensive when you ask | conceals subcontractor status |

---

## questions to ask when someone arrives

1. "what company do you work for?"
2. "are you an employee of [business name] or a subcontractor?"
3. "is your work covered under [business name]'s insurance?"

if answers don't match what you verified, stop and clarify before work begins.

---

## contract language that helps

if you have a written contract, include:

> "all work shall be performed by employees of [Business Name]. if subcontractors are used, Contractor shall provide certificates of insurance for each subcontractor, with Homeowner named as Additional Insured, prior to any subcontractor work."

---

## summary

| situation | your coverage |
|-----------|---------------|
| owner does the work | covered ✓ |
| employee does the work | covered ✓ |
| subcontractor does the work | **not covered** unless separately verified |

the three-layer defense assumes owner/employee. if a subcontractor appears, your defense has a gap. verify or refuse.
