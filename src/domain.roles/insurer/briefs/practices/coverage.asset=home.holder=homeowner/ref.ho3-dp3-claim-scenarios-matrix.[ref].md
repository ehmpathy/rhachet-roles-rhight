# ref.ho3-dp3-claim-scenarios-matrix

## .what

comprehensive matrix of insurance claim types and their coverage outcomes under HO-3 (homeowner) vs DP-3 (landlord) policies when a property is used as a rental.

## .why

- HO-3 policies are designed for owner-occupied residences and exclude rental use
- DP-3 policies are designed for rental/investment properties with different coverage structure
- specific claim scenario awareness prevents coverage gaps and denial surprises
- this matrix documents the "coverage gap" when property use doesn't match policy type

---

## executive summary

| policy | designed for | rental property coverage |
|--------|--------------|-------------------------|
| HO-3 | owner-occupied primary residence | **NO** - claims denied, policy can be rescinded |
| DP-3 | rental/investment property | **YES** - proper coverage for landlord scenarios |

**key principle:** when you rent out a property covered by an HO-3 policy, you violate the owner-occupancy requirement. the insurer can deny claims, rescind the policy retroactively, cancel coverage, or pursue fraud charges.

## part 1: property damage claims

### 1.1 fire damage

| scenario | HO-3 (owner-occupied) | HO-3 (tenant-occupied) | DP-3 (landlord) | key policy language |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **accidental fire** | COVERED | DENIED - occupancy violation | COVERED | HO-3: requires "residence premises" occupied by insured |
| **tenant-caused kitchen fire** | N/A | DENIED - occupancy violation + business activity | COVERED - accidental damage by tenant | DP-3: "covers accidental tenant-caused damage" |
| **tenant deliberately sets fire** | N/A | DENIED | DENIED - intentional act exclusion | both: "intentional loss" exclusion |

**policy language (HO-3):**
> "residence premises must be used principally as a private residence and occupied by the named insured"

**DP-3 coverage note:** all dwelling-fire policies cover tenant damage caused by negligence that results in a fire. even if a fire was accidentally started by your tenant, your landlord policy should provide coverage.

**case example:** Long Island homeowner converted spare unit to rental, kept HO-3 policy. After tenant-caused kitchen fire, carrier denied $180,000 claim due to undisclosed rental use.

---

### 1.2 water damage

| scenario | HO-3 (owner-occupied) | HO-3 (tenant-occupied) | DP-3 (landlord) | notes |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-------|
| **pipe burst (sudden)** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | sudden and accidental = covered |
| **appliance failure** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | water heater, machine overflow |
| **tenant negligence** | N/A | DENIED | NOT COVERED | left faucet on, failed to report leak |
| **gradual leak (unreported)** | LIMITED | DENIED | NOT COVERED | maintenance issue, not sudden event |
| **flood (external)** | NOT COVERED | NOT COVERED | NOT COVERED | requires separate flood policy (NFIP) |
| **sewer/drain backup** | NOT COVERED (unless endorsement) | DENIED | NOT COVERED (unless endorsement) | water backup endorsement available |

**key exclusion:** "most landlord insurance policies will not include flood damage as a covered loss"

**tenant negligence note:** if water damage is caused by the tenant—such as left faucet or failure to report leak—the landlord may not be responsible for the damages under their policy.

| **hail damage** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | may have separate wind/hail deductible |
| **hurricane damage** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | subject to hurricane deductible in coastal areas |

**deductible note:** many policies have separate wind/hail deductibles (often percentage-based) that differ from standard deductible.

### 1.4 theft

| scenario | HO-3 (owner-occupied) | HO-3 (tenant-occupied) | DP-3 (landlord) | notes |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-------|
| **theft by third party** | COVERED (owner's property) | DENIED | COVERED (landlord's property only) | tenant belongings not covered |
| **theft by tenant** | N/A | DENIED | NOT COVERED | theft by insured/tenant excluded |
| **tenant's belongings stolen** | N/A | N/A | NOT COVERED | tenant needs renters insurance (HO-4) |
| **theft in vacant period** | NOT COVERED (after 60 days) | DENIED | NOT COVERED (after vacancy period) | vacancy clause exclusion |

**policy note:** "damages to any tenant's belongings are not considered a covered loss in landlord insurance"

### 1.5 vandalism

| scenario | HO-3 (owner-occupied) | HO-3 (tenant-occupied) | DP-3 (landlord) | notes |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-------|
| **third-party vandalism** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | standard covered peril |
| **tenant vandalism (malicious)** | N/A | DENIED | NOT COVERED | "intentional damage by tenant" excluded |
| **tenant vandalism (accidental)** | N/A | DENIED | COVERED | broken window, etc. |
| **vandalism in vacant period** | NOT COVERED (after 60 days) | DENIED | LIMITED | vacancy clause triggers exclusion |

**key exclusion language:**
> "intentional damage caused by tenants, but not limited to malicious destruction before and while occupied, or within 10 days of eviction or vacancy is excluded"

**important distinction:** damage done by tenants is not considered "vandalism" by insurers because vandalism implies an act by a third party, not someone with authorized access.

### 1.6 electric-storm strikes

| scenario | HO-3 (owner-occupied) | HO-3 (tenant-occupied) | DP-3 (landlord) | notes |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-------|
| **direct strike** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | named peril in all policies |
| **power surge from strike** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | electrical system damage |
| **fire from strike** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | fire is covered peril |
| **appliance damage from surge** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED (if attached appliances) | permanently installed appliances |

**DP-3 coverage includes:** burned/melted wires inside walls, damage to electrical panels and circuit breakers, destroyed HVAC systems or other permanently installed appliances.

### 1.7 smoke damage

| scenario | HO-3 (owner-occupied) | HO-3 (tenant-occupied) | DP-3 (landlord) | notes |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-------|
| **smoke from fire on property** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | consequential damage from covered peril |
| **smoke from adjacent property** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | sudden and accidental |
| **smoke from tenant's stove** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED (if accidental) | normal use, not intentional |

### 1.8 fallen trees/objects

| scenario | HO-3 (owner-occupied) | HO-3 (tenant-occupied) | DP-3 (landlord) | notes |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-------|
| **tree falls on structure** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | wind, electric-storm, hail |
| **tree removal (hit structure)** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | usually up to $500-$1,000 |
| **tree falls, misses structure** | LIMITED ($500-$1,000) | DENIED | LIMITED | most policies have low limits |
| **neighbor's tree falls on property** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | your policy covers your property |

**coverage note:** home insurance typically covers damages caused when a tree on your property falls on your home. homeowners policy usually helps pay for tree removal as well as to fix or replace what the tree crashed into.

## part 2: liability claims

### 2.1 tenant slip and fall

| scenario | HO-3 (owner-occupied) | HO-3 (tenant-occupied) | DP-3 (landlord) | notes |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-------|
| **slip on loose floor** | COVERED (family/guest) | DENIED | COVERED | landlord responsible for maintenance |
| **fall on icy walkway** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED | landlord must maintain common areas |
| **fall due to tenant's negligence** | N/A | DENIED | MAY BE EXCLUDED | depends on lease terms |

**coverage includes:** medical bills, legal defense costs, and damages when a tenant is hurt by a hazard the landlord should have fixed.

**case example (Earl M. Hill v. Trader Joe's, California 2024):** car jumped curb, injured shopper. landlords sued for $2.5 million. CGL policy accepted defense, settled for $1.25 million through insurance.

### 2.3 dog bite liability

| scenario | HO-3 (owner-occupied) | HO-3 (tenant-occupied) | DP-3 (landlord) | notes |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-------|
| **owner's dog bites guest** | COVERED (usually) | DENIED | N/A | some breeds excluded |
| **tenant's dog bites guest** | N/A | DENIED | CONDITIONAL | depends on landlord knowledge |
| **landlord knew dog was dangerous** | N/A | DENIED | COVERED (defense) | landlord liability for failure to act |
| **excluded breed (pit bull, etc.)** | NOT COVERED | DENIED | NOT COVERED | breed-specific exclusions |

**landlord liability triggers:**
- knew about dangerous dog and didn't act
- allowed banned breed on property
- failed to enforce leash rules or pet restrictions
- ignored previous complaints from other tenants

**state variations:**
- **one-bite rule states:** landlords liable only if they knew dog had previously shown aggression
- **strict liability states:** property owners responsible regardless of prior knowledge
- **California:** particularly stringent laws
- **Texas:** landlords liable if they had actual knowledge of dangerous tendencies
- **New York:** requires proof landlord had notice of vicious behavior

**recommended coverage:** $1-$2 million liability per occurrence, plus umbrella policy

### 2.4 lead paint exposure

| scenario | HO-3 (owner-occupied) | HO-3 (tenant-occupied) | DP-3 (landlord) | notes |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-------|
| **tenant claims lead illness** | N/A | DENIED | LIMITED or EXCLUDED | explicit lead exclusion clauses |
| **child diagnosed with elevated lead** | N/A | DENIED | DEFENSE ONLY (maybe) | may have duty to defend |
| **landlord failed to disclose** | N/A | DENIED | NOT COVERED | intentional omission |

**coverage reality:**
> "most commercial general liability (CGL) policies include explicit lead exclusion clauses, which bar coverage for injuries related to lead exposure"

**potential coverage:** some excess liability policies cover lead paint claims even if primary insurance does not. some insurers offer limited coverage (up to $250,000) for abated buildings with unlimited defense costs.

**notice requirement critical:** if a tenant informs the landlord that their child has been diagnosed with lead illness, that is sufficient to trigger the notice requirement. delay in notice—even unintentional—can result in denial.

**tenant damages:** courts can award tenants up to three times damages plus attorneys' fees if landlord knowingly didn't follow Title X disclosure requirements.

### 2.7 wrongful eviction claims

| scenario | HO-3 (owner-occupied) | HO-3 (tenant-occupied) | DP-3 (landlord) | notes |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-------|
| **improper notice given** | N/A | DENIED | COVERED | legal defense + damages |
| **retaliatory eviction** | N/A | DENIED | COVERED | tenant reported code violation |
| **self-help eviction (lockout)** | N/A | DENIED | COVERED | illegal in most states |

**coverage typically includes:**
- legal defense costs and attorney fees
- settlements or court-awarded damages

**coverage limits:** $100,000 to $2 million per occurrence
**deductibles:** typically $250 to $2,500 per claim

**case examples:**
- **California:** improper notice = $25,000 damages + $15,000 attorney fees
- **Texas:** retaliatory lock-out = $40,000 judgment
- **New York:** discriminatory eviction against disabled tenant = $75,000 settlement

**exclusions:**
- intentional or fraudulent acts
- criminal conduct
- rent control ordinance violations
- punitive damages (usually)

## part 3: loss of income claims

### 3.1 lost rent while repairs occur

| scenario | HO-3 (owner-occupied) | HO-3 (tenant-occupied) | DP-3 (landlord) | notes |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-------|
| **property uninhabitable from fire** | PAYS YOUR TEMP HOTEL | DENIED | COVERED - lost rent | DP-3 pays rental income |
| **repairs take 6 months** | COVERED (up to limit) | DENIED | COVERED (6-12 months typical) | policy limits apply |
| **property partially damaged** | COVERED | DENIED | COVERED (proportional) | fair rental value calculation |

**HO-3 "loss of use":** pays for your temporary hotel costs if you can't live in your home.

**DP-3 "loss of rent":** reimburses landlord for rental income lost when property is uninhabitable due to covered peril. typically covers 6-24 months per policy.

## part 4: specific landlord scenarios

### 4.1 tenant causes fire while at stove

| policy | coverage | rationale |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| HO-3 (owner lives there) | COVERED | accidental fire is covered peril |
| HO-3 (property is rented) | DENIED | occupancy violation + business activity |
| DP-3 | COVERED | accidental tenant damage covered |

---

### 4.2 tenant's guest drowns in pool

| policy | coverage | rationale |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| HO-3 (owner lives there) | COVERED (usually) | premises liability |
| HO-3 (property is rented) | DENIED | occupancy violation |
| DP-3 | COVERED (if properly insured) | attractive nuisance doctrine applies |

**critical requirements:**
- pool must be disclosed to insurer (some carriers won't cover pools)
- adequate liability coverage ($1 million+ recommended)
- safety measures (fences, locked gates) required
- lease should assign maintenance responsibility to tenant

**attractive nuisance doctrine:** property owners may be liable for injuries to children who trespass if injury caused by hazardous condition like a pool, even without permission.

---

### 4.3 tenant's belongings stolen

| policy | coverage | rationale |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| HO-3 (owner lives there) | N/A | not applicable |
| HO-3 (property is rented) | DENIED | occupancy violation |
| DP-3 | NOT COVERED | landlord policy covers structure only |
| HO-4 (renters insurance) | COVERED | tenant's policy covers personal property |

**recommendation:** require tenants to carry renters insurance with minimum $20,000 personal property and $100,000 liability coverage.

---

### 4.4 property vacant between tenants

| scenario | HO-3 | DP-3 | notes |
|----------|------|------|-------|
| **vacant 0-30 days** | DENIED (not rental policy) | FULL COVERAGE | normal turnover |
| **vacant 30-60 days** | DENIED | FULL COVERAGE | most policies |
| **vacant 60+ days** | DENIED | LIMITED COVERAGE | vacancy clause triggers |

**vacancy clause effects (60+ days):**
- vandalism: EXCLUDED
- theft: EXCLUDED
- glass breakage: EXCLUDED
- water damage: MAY BE EXCLUDED
- fire: USUALLY STILL COVERED
- wind: USUALLY STILL COVERED

**vacancy vs. unoccupied:**
- **unoccupied:** residents absent but furnishings remain
- **vacant:** all residents and belongings departed; completely empty

**vacancy insurance:** costs 1.5 to 3 times more than standard coverage.

---

### 4.5 property rented on Airbnb for weekend

| policy | coverage | rationale |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| HO-3 (primary residence) | DENIED | business activity exclusion |
| DP-3 (long-term rental) | DENIED | typically excludes short-term rental |
| short-term rental policy | COVERED | designed for vacation rental use |

**case example:** California Airbnb host rented guest house occasionally. tree fell, damaged property. $120,000 claim denied even though property wasn't currently rented and damage had nothing to do with guests. carrier enforced rental exclusion.

**key point:** both HO and DP policies have "business pursuits" exclusions and misrepresentation clauses that void coverage for undisclosed short-term rental use.

---

### 4.6 tenant sues for uninhabitable conditions

| policy | coverage | rationale |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| HO-3 (property is rented) | DENIED | occupancy violation |
| DP-3 | COVERED (defense + liability) | implied warranty of habitability |

**implied warranty of habitability:** landlords must maintain rental property in condition safe and fit for human habitation, even if lease doesn't expressly require repairs.

**tenant remedies:**
- sue for rent paid in uninhabitable period
- costs of alternative accommodation
- property damage to belongings
- emotional distress damages (in some cases)

**DP-3 coverage:**
- property coverage pays for sudden damage repairs
- liability coverage covers legal defense and potential settlements
- helps landlords restore livability quickly and minimize legal exposure

## part 5: case law and claim denial examples

### category A: undisclosed rental activity / material misrepresentation

#### 5.1 Hughes v. First National Insurance Co. (9th Cir. 2024)

**citation:** Hughes v. FNICA, No. 23-55338 (9th Cir. Mar. 20, 2024)

**facts:** policyholder stated property had no business use on application; actually used it frequently for short-term rentals. also omitted three prior documented claims. evidence included reservation statements, rental confirmations, and platform advertisements.

**result:** court upheld rescission of policy.

**key quote:**
> "a material misrepresentation or concealment in an insurance application, whether intentional or unintentional, entitles the insurer to rescind" (California Insurance Code section 331)

**significance:** even innocent misrepresentations justify claim denial if insurer would have rejected application had they known the truth. language barriers and broker reliance are not defenses.

---

#### 5.2 Terrell v. State Farm General Insurance Co. (Cal. Ct. App. 2019)

**citation:** Terrell v. State Farm Gen. Ins. Co., 40 Cal.App.5th 597 (Cal. Ct. App. 2019)

**facts:** homeowners rented their San Francisco property to tenants for 8 years while they had a homeowners policy instead of rental dwelling policy. when a tenant was injured in a porch collapse, State Farm denied the claim.

**result:** court affirmed summary judgment for State Farm. the "business pursuits / rental" exclusion barred coverage.

**key quote:**
> "they were engaged in a regular business activity that generated substantial income over an extended period of time and under written lease terms that would be commonly understood as a property rental."

**significance:** long-term undisclosed rental activity with income generation clearly triggers business pursuits exclusions. wrong policy type for 8+ years resulted in complete coverage denial.

---

#### 5.3 Duarte v. Pacific Specialty Insurance Co. (Cal. Ct. App. 2017)

**citation:** Duarte v. Pac. Specialty Ins. Co., 13 Cal.App.5th 45 (Cal. Ct. App. 2017)

**facts:** landlord applied for landlord-tenant insurance. insurer denied habitability defect claim, said there was material misrepresentation about disputes and business activity on the property.

**result:** reversed summary judgment for insurer. court found application questions were ambiguously worded.

**key quote:**
> the questions were "ambiguously worded and the answers were arguably accurate under one reasonable interpretation."

**significance:** rare win for policyholder. ambiguous application questions may protect insureds from rescission even when rental activity exists. important for rescission challenges.

---

#### 5.4 Jeune v. Peerless Insurance Co. (N.Y. App. Div. 2010)

**citation:** Jeune v. Peerless Ins. Co., 75 A.D.3d 654 (N.Y. App. Div. 3d Dep't 2010)

**facts:** property was insured under a homeowner's policy, but the insureds never moved in after purchase. fire destroyed the home. insurer sought rescission based on misrepresentation about occupancy.

**result:** court denied summary judgment for insurer, found questions of fact about whether misrepresentations were material. plaintiffs had activated utilities and had family members use the premises.

**significance:** occupancy status disputes create factual questions that may preclude summary judgment. partial use of premises can defeat rescission claims.

---

#### 5.5 Universal Property v. Johnson (Florida)

**facts:** homeowners filed claim after residence destroyed in accidental fire. insurer denied claim after investigation revealed misrepresentation on application.

**result:** false information in application sufficient to deny claim even if error was innocent.

**key quote:** relevant consideration is whether insurer's decision to issue policy as written would have been impacted by accurate information.

---

#### 5.6 Mora v. Tower Hill Prime Insurance Co. (Fla. 2d DCA 2015)

**citation:** Mora v. Tower Hill Prime Ins. Co., 155 So.3d 1224 (Fla. 2d DCA 2015)

**facts:** homeowners filed sinkhole claim. insurer sought rescission based on misrepresentation about property defects in application (cracks in pool area and ceilings had been noted in inspection report).

**result:** reversed summary judgment for insurer. insurer failed to establish the misrepresentation was material to acceptance of risk.

**key quote:**
> in cases that lack fraud, explanation for why insurer would not have issued coverage must show "in good faith" and "pursuant to a policy or requirement" it would have acted differently.

**significance:** important Florida precedent that requires insurers to prove actual materiality, not just theoretical impact. may help defeat "gotcha" rescission attempts.

---

### category B: vacancy clause exclusions

#### 5.7 Greene v. Farmers Insurance Exchange (Tex. 2014)

**citation:** Greene v. Farmers Ins. Exch., 446 S.W.3d 761 (Tex. 2014)

**facts:** house in Irving, Texas was vacant for several months when fire spread from adjacent property. homeowner's policy contained clause that suspended dwelling coverage if house was vacant over 60 days.

**result:** Texas Supreme Court upheld coverage denial. vacancy clause was enforceable regardless of whether vacancy was related to cause of loss.

**key quote:**
> the vacancy clause "effectively expand[ed] coverage" to encompass a 60-day period beyond when the homeowner no longer resided in the dwelling. after that, "there was no coverage for the dwelling."

**significance:** major precedent that vacancy clauses are strictly enforced even when vacancy had no connection to the fire. landlords who transition properties face significant gap risks.

---

#### 5.8 New York occupancy case (Flanzig Law)

**facts:** insurer denied coverage because insured never resided in premises—only had intention to live there in future.

**result:** intention to occupy insufficient to meet residency requirement.

**significance:** owner-occupancy means actual residence, not planned future occupancy.

---

### category C: business pursuits / rental exclusions

#### 5.9 Allstate Insurance Co. v. Hallman (Tex. 2005)

**citation:** Allstate Ins. Co. v. Hallman, 159 S.W.3d 640 (Tex. 2005)

**facts:** homeowner leased property for commercial limestone extraction. adjacent property owners sued for damages. Allstate denied coverage under business pursuits exclusion.

**result:** Texas Supreme Court upheld exclusion. no duty to defend or indemnify.

**key quote:**
> a "business pursuit" incorporates "(1) continuity or regularity of the activity, and (2) a profit motive, usually as a means of livelihood, gainful employment, or financial gain."

**significance:** establishes the two-part test for business pursuits: regularity + profit motive. any rental arrangement that generates income likely triggers this exclusion.

---

#### 5.10 Nationwide Mutual Insurance Co. v. Pasiak (Conn. 2017, 2023)

**citation:** Nationwide Mut. Ins. Co. v. Pasiak, 327 Conn. 225 (Conn. 2017); 345 Conn. 255 (Conn. 2023)

**facts:** business owner's employee was held hostage at business location within owner's residence. insurer denied coverage under umbrella policy's business pursuits exclusion.

**result:** Connecticut Supreme Court upheld application of business pursuits exclusion in both appeals.

**significance:** business activity conducted from a residence triggers exclusions even for unrelated incidents. home-based businesses and rental operations face similar exposure.

---

#### 5.11 Richard v. Milazzo (La. Ct. App. 2002)

**citation:** Richard v. Milazzo, 831 So.2d 1055 (La. Ct. App. 2002)

**facts:** confrontation occurred at business location owned by homeowner. homeowner's insurer denied coverage based on business pursuits exclusion.

**result:** court found business pursuits exclusion applied.

**key quote:**
> "the business pursuits exclusion, like other exclusions in a homeowner's policy, is drafted to exclude risks that should be covered by a different policy. similarly, rental dwellings are excluded because rental property insurance covers those risks."

**significance:** clear articulation that rental activity falls outside homeowner policy scope by design, not accident. reinforces need for proper policy type.

---

#### 5.12 Homesite Insurance Co. v. Hindman (N.J. Super. Ct. App. Div. 2010)

**citation:** Homesite Ins. Co. v. Hindman, 413 N.J. Super. 600 (N.J. Super. Ct. App. Div. 2010)

**facts:** homeowner rented portion of home to two boarders for years. when homeowner's dog bit one of them, insurer denied claim under rental exclusion.

**result:** court found rental exclusion did not apply because policy contained exception for 1-2 boarders, and homeowner fell within that exception.

**key quote:**
> "Homesite and Hindman contracted for a homeowner's insurance policy in which one of the risks accepted by Homesite was that which arose from the rental of a portion of the property to one or two boarders."

**significance:** important exception: policies may allow limited rental (1-2 boarders). landlords should verify exact policy language before they assume exclusion applies.

---

### category D: landlord liability / wrong policy type

#### 5.13 Sheckler v. Auto-Owners Insurance Co. (Ill. 2022)

**citation:** Sheckler v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co., 2022 IL 128012 (Ill. 2022)

**facts:** tenants' negligence caused fire at rental property. landlord's insurer paid claim and sought subrogation. third party filed contribution claim against tenants. tenants claimed they were "implied co-insureds" under landlord's policy.

**result:** Illinois Supreme Court ruled tenants are NOT automatically implied co-insureds under landlord's policy. limited prior doctrine to subrogation cases only.

**significance:** clarifies landlord-tenant insurance relationships. tenants cannot claim protection under landlord's policy for their own negligence. each party needs separate coverage.

---

#### 5.14 Earl M. Hill v. Trader Joe's (Cal. 2024)

**facts:** car jumped curb, injured shopper at commercial property. landlords were sued for $2.5 million.

**result:** CGL policy accepted defense, settled for $1.25 million through insurance.

**significance:** demonstrates importance of proper commercial liability coverage for rental/commercial properties.

---

### category E: short-term rental / Airbnb cases

#### 5.15 Richer v. Travelers Insurance (documented lawsuit)

**facts:** Emily Richer filed property damage claim after 120-foot tree fell on roof and caused up to $120,000 in structural damage. Travelers denied claim after it discovered she rented on Airbnb, even though damage did not occur in rental period.

**result:** claim denied based on violation of rental exclusion provision in policy.

**significance:** ANY undisclosed Airbnb activity can void coverage, even for completely unrelated losses. the mere existence of rental use, not its connection to the damage, triggers exclusion.

---

#### 5.16 Citizens Property Insurance rescissions (Fla. multiple cases 2024)

**facts:** in multiple cases, Citizens voided policies from inception after it discovered undisclosed short-term rental use. properties were rented on platforms while insured under standard homeowner policies.

**result:** policies voided ab initio; no payments issued on claims.

**significance:** Citizens actively investigates rental activity and rescinds policies. Florida landlords face heightened scrutiny from state's largest insurer.

---

### category F: bad faith / insurer conduct

#### 5.17 Bello v. Merrimack Mutual Fire Insurance Co. (N.J. App. Div.)

**facts:** storm damaged roof and wall. firm concluded wall wasn't properly maintained. insurer denied entire claim.

**result:** jury verdict for policyholder on bad faith claim. damages exceeded $850,000 over policy limit of $100,750.

**significance:** wrongful denials can result in catastrophic liability for insurers. policyholders should understand bad faith claims as leverage, but this doesn't help if policy type was fundamentally wrong.

---

#### 5.18 Brito v. Citizens Property Insurance Corp. (Fla. 2d DCA)

**facts:** homeowners filed wind damage claim. Citizens denied claim entirely and claimed damage was wear and tear. dispute arose over proper measure of damages after initial denial.

**result:** Second District Court of Appeal sided with policyholders, reversed trial court.

**significance:** complete claim denials may backfire on insurers. when coverage is wrongly denied entirely, policyholders may recover more than under partial denial scenarios.

### case law summary table

| issue | insurer wins when | policyholder wins when |
|-------|-------------------|------------------------|
| material misrepresentation | clear false answers about rental use | ambiguous questions, disputed facts |
| vacancy clause | property vacant 60+ days | partial occupancy, utilities active |
| business pursuits | regular activity + profit motive | occasional/incidental activity |
| rental exclusion | property rented to tenants | within boarder exception (1-2) |
| rescission | proven materiality to risk | insurer fails to show actual impact |

## summary: the coverage gap

when a property is used as a rental but insured with an HO-3 homeowner's policy:

| claim type | result |
|------------|--------|
| property damage | DENIED - occupancy violation |
| liability claims | DENIED - business activity exclusion |
| loss of rent | DENIED - not designed for rental |
| any claim | POLICY MAY BE RESCINDED retroactively |

**the solution:** convert to DP-3 landlord insurance BEFORE you rent. the 25% higher premium is the cost of actual coverage.
