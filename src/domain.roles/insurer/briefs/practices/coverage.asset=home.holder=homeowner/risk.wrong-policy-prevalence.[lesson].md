# risk.wrong-policy-prevalence

## .what

how common it is for rental property investors to have homeowners insurance instead of landlord insurance, and why this problem persists.

## .why

- the problem is more prevalent than people realize
- systemic gaps in the closing process allow it to happen
- understanding why it persists helps avoid the trap
- tax records are public, but insurers only check them at claim time

---

## prevalence: majority of rentals have wrong insurance

> "the majority of rental properties are written as homeowner's policies rather than rental dwelling policies."
>
> — [Trust ETC](https://www.trustetc.com/blog/costs-wrong-rental-property-insurance/)

this is not a rare edge case. it is the default state for rental properties.

### supporting evidence

| source | finding |
|--------|---------|
| [Trust ETC](https://www.trustetc.com/blog/costs-wrong-rental-property-insurance/) | majority of rentals have HO not DP |
| [Richey Insurance](https://richeyinsurance.com/landlord-insurance-statistics/) | 70% of residential properties underinsured |
| [BiggerPockets forums](https://www.biggerpockets.com/forums/95/topics/1175124-landlords-vs-homeowners-insurance) | 10+ threads from confused new landlords |
| [Cribb Insurance](https://www.cribbinsurance.com/avoid-these-common-landlord-insurance-claim-mistakes/) | "assuming homeowner's will cover rental is common mistake" |

### why the forums are full of this question

search BiggerPockets for "homeowners vs landlord insurance" and you'll find dozens of threads:

- "landlords vs homeowners insurance"
- "homeowner vs landlord insurance?"
- "landlord ins. vs homeowner's ins. for long term rental"
- "homeowners insurance and landlord insurance???"

the question recurs because new investors keep making the same mistake — and the industry does not stop them.

## why the closing process does not catch this

### the referral pipeline has no safety check

when you buy an investment property:

1. **realtor** refers you to their preferred lender
2. **lender** requires proof of insurance
3. **realtor** refers you to their preferred insurance agent
4. **insurance agent** sells you a policy
5. **title company** verifies insurance exists
6. **closing** happens

**missing step: no one verifies the policy type matches the property use.**

| party | responsibility | checks policy type? |
|-------|----------------|---------------------|
| realtor | facilitate sale | no |
| lender | verify insurance exists | no |
| insurance agent | sell policy | should, often doesn't |
| title company | verify insurance exists | no |
| escrow | collect documents | no |

### RESPA does not apply to investment properties

the Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (RESPA) protects owner-occupant homebuyers. but RESPA does not apply to:

- cash sales
- investment properties
- commercial real estate
- non-federally-related mortgages

this means investment property transactions have fewer consumer protections than primary residence purchases.

### insurance agents work on commission

a homeowners policy and a landlord policy pay similar commissions. the agent has no financial incentive to ask probing questions about occupancy. if you don't volunteer that it's a rental, they may not ask.

## why don't insurers check tax records upfront?

### the asymmetric investigation problem

insurers check tax records — but only **after** you file a claim.

> "once a misrepresentation is identified, a thorough investigation by the claims adjuster—such as obtaining tax filings, rental permits, prior insurance applications, and housing inspections made prior to purchase or mortgage refinancing—can be very useful."
>
> — [Dick Law Firm](https://www.dicklawfirm.com/blog/2023/august/what-happens-if-i-fail-to-disclose-rental-activi/)

**at underwriting**: insurers rely on your application. they do not verify.

**at claim time**: insurers investigate everything. they pull tax records, rental permits, utility bills, mail forwarding records.

### why insurers don't check upfront

| reason | explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **cost** | checking tax records for every policy is expensive |
| **premium collection** | they collect premiums either way |
| **denial leverage** | discovering misrepresentation at claim time lets them deny |
| **fraud deterrent** | the threat of denial deters some fraud |
| **application reliance** | you signed the application, you're liable for accuracy |

### the perverse incentive

insurers benefit from this system:

1. they collect premiums from misrepresented policies
2. they can deny claims when misrepresentation is discovered
3. they face no obligation to verify upfront
4. the burden of accuracy is on the applicant

## why isn't this louder knowledge?

### who would amplify it?

| party | motivation to publicize | actually publicizes? |
|-------|------------------------|---------------------|
| insurers | none — they benefit from status quo | no |
| realtors | none — complicates sales | no |
| lenders | none — not their problem | no |
| insurance agents | some — could upsell DP | rarely |
| denied claimants | high — but isolated individuals | occasionally, forums |
| consumer advocates | high | yes, but limited reach |

### the silence of denied claimants

when a claim is denied:

- the claimant is embarrassed (they signed a false application)
- the claimant may face fraud investigation
- the claimant has no platform
- the claimant often settles quietly

denied claimants don't become advocates. they disappear.

### survivorship bias in investor education

successful landlords have proper insurance. they share advice like "get landlord insurance." but they don't share horror stories because they don't have horror stories.

the people with horror stories:
- lost their property
- faced bankruptcy
- were investigated for fraud
- don't want to talk about it

## the tax record question

### why tax records should catch this

when you convert a property to rental:

1. you file Schedule E (rental income) on your taxes
2. you may lose homestead exemption
3. the county assessor may reclassify the property
4. these records are public

**in theory**, anyone could check:
- is this property claimed as owner-occupied for insurance?
- is this property filed as rental income on taxes?

**in practice**, no one does this check until you file a claim.

### the Schedule E / HO-3 contradiction

| document | says |
|----------|------|
| IRS Schedule E | this property earns rental income |
| HO-3 application | i live here as my primary residence |

these documents contradict each other. both are signed under penalty of perjury. yet no one cross-references them until a fire happens.

## who is at risk?

### high-risk profiles

| profile | why at risk |
|---------|-------------|
| **first-time investor** | doesn't know HO ≠ DP |
| **house hacker** | bought as primary, now rents rooms |
| **accidental landlord** | inherited property, rents it out |
| **DIY investor** | skipped insurance agent, bought online |
| **realtor referral buyer** | trusted the referral pipeline |

### the realtor referral trap

the user's exact scenario is common:

1. buy investment property with mortgage
2. realtor refers insurance agent
3. agent sells HO-3 (same policy they sell everyone)
4. no one asks "will you live here?"
5. property is rented
6. claim happens
7. denied

the realtor's insurance referral is optimized for primary residence buyers. investment property buyers get the same referral, with the same policy, for a different use case.

## what should change (but won't)

| reform | barrier |
|--------|---------|
| require policy type verification at closing | costs money, no one pays |
| cross-reference tax records at underwriting | insurers benefit from status quo |
| realtor liability for wrong insurance referral | RESPA doesn't apply to investments |
| insurance agent duty to verify occupancy | would reduce sales |
| mandatory disclosure at claim time | already exists, problem is upfront |

---

## what you can do

1. **never trust the referral pipeline** — verify policy type yourself
2. **read your policy** — look for "owner-occupied" or "primary residence" language
3. **disclose rental intent** — even if not asked
4. **get DP-3 before first tenant** — not after
5. **check annually** — occupancy changes require policy changes
