# define.conditional-coverage-repair-window

## .what

some carriers issue coverage with a 30-60-90 day window to fix hazards like K&T wiring before cancellation. this is called **conditional coverage** or **coverage pending repairs**.

## .why

> **carriers allow temporary coverage because refusing entirely loses the customer. conditional acceptance captures premium while managing risk.**

---

## why carriers allow the repair window

### business rationale

| factor | insurer benefit |
|--------|-----------------|
| premium capture | collect premiums during remediation period |
| risk reduction | property becomes fully insurable after fix |
| customer retention | maintains relationship vs. outright denial |
| competitive positioning | differentiates from carriers that refuse entirely |
| controlled exposure | limited time window caps potential loss period |

### the economics

refusing all properties with hazards eliminates a significant market segment. many pre-1970 homes have some obsolete electrical component. by allowing conditional coverage:

- carrier writes the policy (revenue)
- property converts from high-risk to acceptable-risk (win-win)
- paying for quick repairs is cheaper than paying catastrophic claims

> "Most insurance companies require homeowners to convert to modern electrical standards within 30 days of purchase."
>
> — Insure.com

---

## typical timeframes

| timeframe | common use cases |
|-----------|------------------|
| **30 days** | K&T removal, critical electrical hazards, new purchase requirements |
| **60 days** | Florida statutory limit; panel replacement |
| **90 days** | non-critical upgrades, service amperage improvements |

### Steadily example

Steadily will cover older homes but requires a 4-point inspection (roof, HVAC, plumbing, electrical). if K&T is found:

1. coverage is conditional
2. property owner has 30 days to remediate
3. if not fixed: policy canceled

> "In some cases, companies will allow from 30 to 60 days after policy inception to have it professionally removed."
>
> — Honeycomb Insurance

## legal framework

### Florida statute 627.4133

Florida explicitly allows cancellation for failure to meet underwriting requirements:

> "After the policy has been in effect for 60 days, the policy may not be canceled by the insurer unless there has been... a failure to comply, within 60 days after the date of effectuation of coverage, with underwriting requirements established by the insurer before the date of effectuation of coverage."
>
> — [Florida Statute 627.4133](https://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0600-0699/0627/Sections/0627.4133.html)

### cancellation notice requirements by state

| state | notice requirement |
|-------|-------------------|
| Florida | 20 days (first 60 days); 45-120 days (after) |
| New York | 45-60 days before expiration for nonrenewal |
| Wisconsin | 10 days minimum; 60 days for nonrenewal |
| California | various requirements in Insurance Code 660-679.7 |

**important**: the 30-60-90 day repair windows are **underwriting requirements**, not state-mandated grace periods. state laws protect consumers by requiring written notice before cancellation.

## what happens if the fix is not completed

| stage | what happens |
|-------|-------------|
| initial notice | insurer specifies repair requirement and deadline |
| deadline passes | insurer sends cancellation or nonrenewal notice |
| cancellation notice | 10-45 day notice period (varies by state) |
| policy terminated | coverage ends; lender notified |
| mortgage impact | lender may force-place insurance (5-10x normal premiums) |

### mid-term cancellation vs nonrenewal

| type | when it happens | severity |
|------|-----------------|----------|
| mid-term cancellation | before policy expires | critical hazard |
| nonrenewal | at policy expiration | more common for property-condition issues |

> "Not completing the repair in time, and not being communicative with your insurer, could lead to your policy being canceled mid-term or your insurer may choose to not renew your policy."
>
> — Reviews.com

## the 4-point inspection

for homes 20-30+ years old (especially in Florida), carriers require a 4-point inspection:

| system | what is checked |
|--------|----------------|
| electrical | panel brand, K&T, service amperage, visible defects |
| roof | age, condition |
| plumbing | pipe material, water heater |
| HVAC | age, condition |

### automatic failures (coverage denied or conditional)

| finding | typical outcome |
|---------|----------------|
| Federal Pacific panel | denied |
| Zinsco panel | denied |
| K&T active | denied or 30-day conditional |
| aluminum (unmitigated) | denied or surcharge |
| fuse box | upgrade required |

there is no "pass/fail" — instead, issues result in:
- higher premiums
- coverage exclusions
- requirements to complete repairs before coverage
- conditional coverage for 30-60 days to allow repairs

## strategic implications for landlords

### if offered conditional coverage

1. **take it** — temporary coverage is better than no coverage
2. **get repair quotes immediately** — electrician inspection on day 1
3. **communicate progress** — carriers may extend deadline if you show progress
4. **document everything** — photos, receipts, electrician letters

### if denied entirely

conditional coverage is actually a favorable outcome. it means:
- carrier is willing to insure the property
- hazard is fixable, not disqualifying
- you have a clear path to standard coverage

outright denial means surplus lines or FAIR plan.
