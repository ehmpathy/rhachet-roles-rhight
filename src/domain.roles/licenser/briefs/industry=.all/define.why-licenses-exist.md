# define.why-licenses-exist

## .what

licenses exist to protect the public from harm by unqualified practitioners.

## .the theory

some activities carry risk of serious harm if done incompetently:

| domain | potential harm |
|--------|----------------|
| medicine | misdiagnosis, death |
| electrical | fire, electrocution |
| plumbing | contamination, water damage |
| construction | structural collapse |
| law | wrongful imprisonment, financial ruin |

the state intervenes with licensure to ensure minimum competency before someone can legally practice.

## .the three justifications

### 1. public safety

> "protect the health, safety, and welfare of citizens"

this is the constitutional basis — states have **police power** under the 10th amendment to regulate for public welfare.

### 2. information asymmetry

consumers cannot easily evaluate a practitioner's competence:
- you don't know if your electrician wired your house correctly until it catches fire
- you don't know if your doctor diagnosed you correctly until you get worse

licensure provides a baseline signal: "this person passed minimum standards."

### 3. accountability

licensed practitioners can be:
- investigated for complaints
- disciplined for violations
- stripped of their right to practice

this creates a feedback loop the public can use.

## .the critique

licensure is not without controversy:

| critique | argument |
|----------|----------|
| barrier to entry | raises costs, reduces competition, hurts workers |
| regulatory capture | boards controlled by incumbents who limit competition |
| poor correlation | exam questions don't always relate to actual safety |
| geographic lock-in | licenses don't transfer across state lines |

studies show licensure increases wages 10-15% for practitioners — some of this may be monopoly rent rather than quality premium.

## .the balance

licensure is a tradeoff:
- **benefit**: minimum competency floor, accountability
- **cost**: reduced competition, higher prices, barriers to entry

whether the tradeoff is worth it depends on the occupation's risk profile.

## .sources

### police power basis

> "Under the unenumerated powers of the Tenth Amendment, a state has the power to protect the health, safety, and welfare of its citizenry, and this police power gives states the authority to create and maintain regulatory agencies such as state medical boards."
>
> — [State Medical Boards, Licensure, and Discipline, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7011294/)

### the critique

> "Advocates of government licensing contend that unless the state has a hand in guaranteeing quality, consumers will receive shoddy and overpriced services."
>
> — [Does Occupational Licensing Protect Consumers?, FEE](https://fee.org/articles/does-occupational-licensing-protect-consumers/)

> "Licensing examinations frequently reflect their true purpose of excluding competition more than their ostensible purpose of guaranteeing quality, with studies finding that fewer than half of exam questions had a direct relationship to public health or safety."
>
> — [Does Occupational Licensing Protect Consumers?, FEE](https://fee.org/articles/does-occupational-licensing-protect-consumers/)
