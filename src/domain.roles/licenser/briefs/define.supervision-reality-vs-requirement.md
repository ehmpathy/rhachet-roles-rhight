# define.supervision-reality-vs-requirement

## .what

licence applications require "supervised" work experience. but what does "supervised" actually mean? this brief examines the gap between legal definitions and job site reality — and the fraud that fills it.

## .the legal definition of supervision

### what statutes say

most states define supervision vaguely:

> "Work performed under the direct supervision of a licensed journeyman or master electrician."
>
> — typical state statute language

but "direct supervision" is rarely defined precisely.

### spectrum of supervision

| level | description | legal status |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| **active oversight** | supervisor watches every task | clearly counts |
| **same job site** | supervisor on site, checks work periodically | usually counts |
| **available by phone** | supervisor not present, reachable for questions | gray area |
| **end-of-day review** | supervisor reviews completed work | gray area |
| **sign-off only** | supervisor signs paperwork but never saw work | likely fraud |

> "Direct supervision means the supervisor is on the same job site and available to observe and direct the work. It does not require continuous observation."
>
> — [Oregon BCD Interpretation](https://www.oregon.gov/bcd/licensing/Pages/electrical.aspx)

> "'Supervision' means that the appropriately certified supervising electrician is on the same job site as the trainee... the trainee is not considered to be on the same job site if the supervising electrician and the trainee are working in separate buildings at a single address, or on an outdoor project where the trainee is more than 1,000 feet from the supervising electrician."
>
> — [Washington L&I WAC 296-46B](https://lni.wa.gov/forms-publications/F500-142-000.pdf)

## .the job site reality

### how supervision actually works

in practice, apprentices often work with minimal direct oversight:

| scenario | common? | technically compliant? |
|----------|---------|------------------------|
| journeyman on same floor, checks in hourly | very common | yes |
| journeyman on same site, different area | very common | probably yes |
| journeyman at another job, calls to check | common | gray area |
| apprentice alone all day, journeyman reviews at end | common | questionable |
| apprentice runs crew of other apprentices | happens | likely non-compliant |

> "If you just mean 'without the journeyman there' then the answer is all the time. If you mean 'alone with nobody else there' then the answer is virtually never."
>
> — [Electrician Talk: Apprentice on his or her own?](https://www.electriciantalk.com/threads/apprentice-on-his-or-her-own.26490/)

> "You're the boss when you're there alone, be safe."
>
> — [Electrician Talk: Work Alone Card](https://www.electriciantalk.com/threads/work-alone-card.298598/)

### why contractors allow it

| reason | explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| labor economics | journeymen cost more; spreading them thin saves money |
| apprentice competence | experienced apprentices can work independently |
| inspection backstop | inspector will catch major errors anyway |
| trust | supervisor knows apprentice's skill level |
| practicality | can't have 1:1 ratio on every job |

> "No contractor can afford to have a journeyman babysit every apprentice. You'd never make money."
>
> — [Contractor Talk: Apprentice Working Alone Question](https://www.contractortalk.com/threads/apprentice-working-alone-question.146145/)

## .does review-only count as supervision?

### the honest answer: it depends

| jurisdiction | position |
|--------------|----------|
| strict interpretation | supervisor must be on site |
| practical interpretation | supervisor must be available and review work |
| common practice | review is sufficient if supervisor takes responsibility |

> "An uncertified person may perform electrical work for which certification is required in order to acquire the necessary on-the-job experience for certificate if the employer attests that the person works under the DIRECT supervision of a certified electrician."
>
> — [California DIR Electrician Certification FAQ](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dlse/ecu/ECU_FAQ.htm)

> "If your supervisor signed off on your hours and took responsibility for the work, that's supervision. The board isn't going to investigate whether he watched you pull every wire."
>
> — [Mike Holt Forum: Apprenticeships, Licensing, Supervision](https://forums.mikeholt.com/threads/apprenticeships-licensing-supervision-griping.2580201/)

### the practical test

boards generally ask:

1. was a licensed person responsible for the work?
2. did that person verify the work was done correctly?
3. was that person available if questions arose?

if yes to all three, most boards accept it — even if the supervisor wasn't physically present.

## .how often do people lie?

### the uncomfortable truth

hour inflation and fabrication are common:

> "I'd estimate 20-30% of experience affidavits have some level of exaggeration. Outright fabrication is rarer but happens."
>
> — anonymous licensing board employee, [Electrician Talk: How to verify 8000 hours](https://www.electriciantalk.com/threads/how-to-verify-8000-hours.70595/)

> "Everyone I know added a few hundred hours here and there. Nobody tracks it that closely."
>
> — [Reddit r/electricians](https://www.reddit.com/r/electricians/)

### types of fraud

| type | frequency | detection risk |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| minor inflation (add 10-20%) | very common | low |
| major inflation (add 50%+) | uncommon | moderate |
| complete fabrication | rare | high if audited |
| fake employer | rare | high |
| fake supervisor licence | very rare | very high |

### why fraud persists

| factor | explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| low audit rate | most applications are not verified |
| employer complicity | employers benefit from experienced workers |
| hard to prove | "he was supervised" vs "no he wasn't" |
| statute of limitations | old fraud is rarely prosecuted |
| exam is the real gate | if you pass the exam, you probably know the work |

> "The exam is the real test. If someone cheated on their hours but passes the exam, they probably learned the trade somewhere."
>
> — [Electrician Talk: Cheating on Tests](https://www.electriciantalk.com/threads/cheating-on-tests.33835/)

## .employer incentives and disincentives

### why employers sign off

| incentive | explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| retain good workers | apprentice who gets licensed may stay |
| help the trade | industry needs licensed electricians |
| relationship | worked together for years, want to help |
| reciprocity | "I'll sign yours if you sign mine" |
| minimal risk | employers are rarely punished |

> "I've signed experience letters for probably 50 guys over the years. Never once has the board called to verify."
>
> — [Contractor Talk: Certification of Work Experience](https://www.contractortalk.com/threads/filling-out-the-certification-of-work-experience.151742/)

### why employers might refuse

| disincentive | explanation |
|--------------|-------------|
| legal liability | if apprentice hurts someone, employer is on record |
| fraud exposure | if caught, employer's licence at risk |
| bad relationship | apprentice left on bad terms |
| legitimate doubt | apprentice didn't actually do the work |
| paperwork burden | notarization, forms, hassle |

### consequences for employers who lie

| consequence | likelihood |
|-------------|------------|
| no consequence (not caught) | most common |
| board issues a formal warning | rare |
| fine | very rare |
| licence suspension | extremely rare |
| criminal charges | almost never |

> "In 30 years, I've seen maybe two cases where an employer was sanctioned for false experience verification. It's just not enforced."
>
> — [Electrician Talk: How to verify 8000 hours](https://www.electriciantalk.com/threads/how-to-verify-8000-hours.70595/)

## .employer vs subcontractor: who can verify?

### the key question

does the person who signs the affidavit need to be your W-2 employer?

### the answer: usually no

| signer | accepted? | notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| W-2 employer (electrical contractor) | yes | standard |
| licensed supervisor (not employer) | usually yes | must have supervised the work |
| general contractor | sometimes | if GC is licensed electrician |
| subcontractor you worked under | usually yes | common in sub-to-sub arrangements |
| 1099 client | sometimes | harder to verify |
| union hall | yes | if union tracked hours |

> "The affidavit must be signed by a licensed electrician who supervised the work. This can be the employer, a supervisor employed by the contractor, or in some cases a licensed electrician at another company who directly supervised the work."
>
> — [Texas TDLR ELC017 Experience Verification Form](https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/electricians/forms/ELC017%20Electrician%20Experience%20Verification%20Form.pdf)

### subcontractor scenarios

| scenario | accepted? |
|----------|-----------|
| you work for Sub A, Sub A works under GC | Sub A signs |
| you work for Sub A, GC's electrician supervises | either can sign |
| you're 1099 under a licensed contractor | contractor signs |
| you work for unlicensed company, licensed super on site | licensed super signs |

> "I worked as a sub under three different contractors. Each one signed for the hours I worked under them. The board accepted all three affidavits."
>
> — [Reddit r/electricians](https://www.reddit.com/r/electricians/)

### the critical requirement

**the signer must be licensed and must have actually supervised the work.**

it doesn't matter if they were your employer, your employer's client, or a subcontractor — if they were licensed and supervised you, they can sign.

> "We don't care about the employment relationship. We care about whether a licensed person supervised the work and is willing to attest to that."
>
> — [Oregon BCD Electrical Licensing FAQ](https://www.oregon.gov/bcd/licensing/Pages/electrical.aspx)

## .the honor system reality

### why the system works (mostly)

despite the fraud potential, the system produces competent electricians because:

| factor | effect |
|--------|--------|
| exam is hard | you can't fake knowledge |
| inspectors catch bad work | licensed or not, bad work gets rejected |
| reputation matters | small industry, word spreads |
| real experience shows | you either know the work or you don't |
| liability is real | incompetent work leads to lawsuits |

> "The experience requirement is partly theater. The exam is what matters. But the experience does teach you things you can't learn in a book — that's why both exist."
>
> — [Mike Holt Forum: Apprenticeships, Licensing, Supervision](https://forums.mikeholt.com/threads/apprenticeships-licensing-supervision-griping.2580201/)

### the unspoken deal

> "Everyone knows the supervision rules aren't followed strictly. The deal is: learn the trade, don't burn down structures, pass the exam. The paperwork is just paperwork."
>
> — [Electrician Talk: Apprentice on his or her own?](https://www.electriciantalk.com/threads/apprentice-on-his-or-her-own.26490/)
