# define.guild-model-history

## .what

the apprentice → journeyman → master progression used in electrician licensure descends directly from medieval European craft guilds. this brief traces that lineage.

## .etymology

### guild

from Old English *gild* ("payment, tribute") and Old Norse *gildi* ("guild, brotherhood"). the root sense is **mutual contribution** — members paid into a common fund for shared benefit.

> "guild (n.): early 13c., from Old Norse *gildi* 'guild, payment, tribute'"
>
> — Online Etymology Dictionary

### apprentice

from Old French *aprentis* ("learner"), from *aprendre* ("to learn"), from Latin *apprehendere* ("to grasp, seize"). an apprentice is one who **grasps** a trade.

> "apprentice (n.): early 14c., 'one bound by legal agreement to serve another in order to learn a craft'"
>
> — Online Etymology Dictionary

### journeyman

from Old French *journee* ("a day's work, a day's travel"). a journeyman was paid **by the day** and could travel between masters to find work. not yet a master, but no longer bound as an apprentice.

> "journeyman (n.): 'one who has learned a trade and works for daily wages'"
>
> — Online Etymology Dictionary

### master

from Latin *magister* ("chief, director, teacher"), from *magis* ("more"). the master has **more** skill and authority — can teach others and run a shop.

## .medieval guild structure (1100-1500 AD)

### the three stages

| stage | duration | status | rights |
|-------|----------|--------|--------|
| apprentice | 5-9 years | bound to master | room, board, instruction |
| journeyman | variable | free to travel | daily wage, seek masters |
| master | lifetime | guild member | own shop, hire apprentices |

### how it worked

1. **apprentice** — a boy (typically age 12-14) was bound by contract (*indenture*) to a master for 5-9 years. the master provided food, shelter, and instruction. the apprentice provided labor.

2. **journeyman** — after the apprenticeship, the worker was free. he traveled (*journeyed*) to work for various masters, learn different techniques, and earn daily wages. this could last years.

3. **master** — to attain master status, a journeyman produced a *masterpiece* — a work that proved competence. the guild examined it. if accepted, he could open his own shop and take apprentices.

### guild functions

| function | purpose |
|----------|---------|
| quality control | set standards, inspect work, punish fraud |
| price control | prevent undercut prices, ensure fair wages |
| entry control | limit competition, protect members |
| welfare | support sick members, widows, orphans |
| secrets | guard trade knowledge from outsiders |

> "The guilds regulated the quality and price of goods, the conditions of apprenticeship, and the requirements to attain master status."
>
> — Encyclopedia Britannica

## .transfer to modern trades

### why the model survived

the guild model persists in electrical, plumber, and other trades because:

| medieval concern | modern equivalent |
|------------------|-------------------|
| dangerous work (blacksmith burns) | dangerous work (electrocution) |
| quality matters (sword that breaks kills) | quality matters (bad wires cause fires) |
| knowledge is experiential | you cannot learn to wire from a book |
| public trust required | licensure protects consumers |

### the transformation path

| era | system | authority |
|-----|--------|-----------|
| 1100-1500 | craft guilds | guild hall |
| 1500-1800 | guild decline | still guilds, weakened |
| 1800-1900 | industrialization | factories replace guilds |
| 1880-1920 | trade unions rise | unions inherit guild functions |
| 1900-present | state licensure | government codifies guild model |

### unions as guild successors

trade unions (especially IBEW for electricians, founded 1891) inherited guild functions:

| guild function | union equivalent |
|----------------|------------------|
| apprenticeship | union apprenticeship programs |
| journeyman status | journeyman card |
| quality standards | union work standards |
| entry control | union membership requirements |
| mutual aid | union benefits, pension |

> "The International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers was founded in 1891 to organize electrical workers and establish standards for the trade."
>
> — IBEW History

### state licensure codifies the model

governments formalized what guilds once controlled:

| guild controlled | state now controls |
|------------------|-------------------|
| who can practice | licence requirements |
| quality standards | codes (NEC) |
| apprenticeship terms | hour requirements |
| journeyman recognition | journeyman licence |
| master recognition | master/contractor licence |

## .the three stages today

### apprentice (modern)

| aspect | medieval | modern |
|--------|----------|--------|
| duration | 5-9 years | 4-5 years |
| entry | family connection | application |
| bound to | one master | employer or program |
| compensation | room and board | hourly wage |
| classroom | none formal | 144-200 hours/year |

### journeyman (modern)

| aspect | medieval | modern |
|--------|----------|--------|
| travel | required (journeyed) | optional |
| licence | guild recognition | state/local licence |
| authority | work for any master | work for any contractor |
| pay | daily wage | hourly/salary |

### master (modern)

| aspect | medieval | modern |
|--------|----------|--------|
| proof | masterpiece | additional exam |
| authority | own shop | pull permits, supervise |
| apprentices | could train | can supervise apprentices |
| business | own business | contractor licence |

## .why the model endures

the apprentice-journeyman-master model has survived 900+ years because:

1. **danger requires supervised practice** — you cannot learn dangerous work from books alone
2. **skill requires time** — 4-5 years is not arbitrary; it takes that long to encounter enough situations
3. **quality requires gatekeepers** — someone must verify competence before independent practice
4. **consumers need protection** — bad electrical work kills; licensure protects the public

> "The essential features of the medieval guild system — apprenticeship, journeyman status, and master certification — survive in modern trade licensure because they address fundamental problems of skill transmission and quality assurance in dangerous work."
>
> — paraphrased from various labor history sources
