# Domain Terminology Research: Permit-Required Determination System

## Executive Summary

This research analyzes terminology options for five core domain objects in a permit-required determination system. We examined 25+ sources across legal, medical, regulatory, engineering, culinary, maritime, and software domains to identify terms that maximize **specificity** (eliminate ambiguity) and **intuition** (minimize friction, maximize adoption).

---

## 1. The Permit Requirement Determination Result

**Question:** What do we call the output of "is this work permit-required?"

### Candidate Terms

| Term | Domain Origin | Pros | Cons |
|------|---------------|------|------|
| **Determination** | Administrative Law, SSA, EPA, IRS | Official government term; implies authority and finality | Generic; could be confused with the process |
| **Ruling** | Courts, Administrative Law | Clear decision connotation | Implies judicial authority; may feel adversarial |
| **Finding** | Courts, Medical | Good for factual conclusions | Often plural; sounds investigative |
| **Verdict** | Criminal Courts | Strong finality | Too adversarial; implies guilt/innocence |
| **Assessment** | Medical, Compliance | Broad, inclusive | Too soft; implies evaluation that continues |
| **Opinion** | IRS, Courts | Precedent in tax law | Suggests subjectivity, not authority |

### Research Evidence

**[1] Cornell Law - Legal Information Institute** [official]
https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/administrative_law
> "A determination refers to a final decision made by a court or an administrative agency on a specific issue."

**[2] IRS - Determination Letters** [official]
https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/determination-opinion-and-advisory-letters
> "A favorable determination letter expresses the IRS' opinion that the plan's terms meet the Internal Revenue Code's tax-qualification requirements."

**[3] EPA - Applicability Determinations** [official]
https://www.epa.gov/caa-permitting/applicability-determination-requests
> "An applicability determination is a formal EPA response to questions about how regulations apply to a particular situation."

**[4] SSA - Disability Determination Process** [official]
https://www.ssa.gov/disability/determination.htm
> "The DDSs (Disability Determination Services) are State agencies responsible for medical evidence development and the initial determination on whether or not a claimant is disabled or blind under the law."

**[5] Library of Congress - Administrative Decisions** [official]
https://guides.loc.gov/administrative-law/decisions
> "Findings are the explanation that review courts need to determine whether substantial evidence exists... a factual basis for jurisdiction, findings as to each element of each rule violation that is found, and findings of fact adequate to support the original complaint."

**[6] LawProse - Terminology Distinctions** [practitioner]
https://lawprose.org/lawprose-lesson-165-ruling-vs-opinion-vs-judgment-etc/
> "A decision is the outcome of a court's decision, whether on some particular point of law or on the case as a whole... An opinion is a court's written statement of the relevant facts, the applicable points of law, the logic that led to the court's decision."

**[7] Encyclopedia.com - Assessment and Diagnosis** [academic]
https://www.encyclopedia.com/medicine/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/assessment-and-diagnosis
> "Assessment is the process of evaluation of a patient's symptoms, medical history, and other relevant information to determine the best course of action... diagnosis is the identification of a specific disease or condition based on the results of an assessment."

### Convergence Signal

Sources [1], [2], [3], [4] independently use "determination" as the official government term for formal decisions on applicability, eligibility, or compliance. This represents strong convergence across administrative law, tax, environmental, and disability contexts.

### Recommendation

**Primary Term: `Determination`**

Rationale:
- Used by EPA, IRS, SSA, and administrative law as the standard term for official decisions
- "Applicability determination" (EPA) directly parallels "permit requirement determination"
- Implies authority without adversarial connotation
- Clear noun form; unambiguous

**Rejected alternatives:**
- "Ruling" - too judicial; implies adversarial proceeding
- "Finding" - typically plural; implies investigation rather than decision
- "Assessment" - too soft; implies process that continues rather than conclusion
- "Verdict" - criminal law connotation; inappropriate for regulatory context

## 2. The Proof Chain That Supports the Determination

**Question:** What do we call the evidence trail from code baseline to exemptions to jurisdiction?

### Candidate Terms

| Term | Domain Origin | Pros | Cons |
|------|---------------|------|------|
| **Chain of Custody** | Forensics, Legal | Strong integrity connotation | Implies physical evidence handling |
| **Audit Trail** | Accounts, Compliance | Well-understood; implies traceability | More about record-creation than logic |
| **Rationale** | Legal, Philosophy | Focuses on logic | Doesn't convey chain/sequence |
| **Proof Chain** | Novel | Explicit about proof + sequence | Not established terminology |
| **Evidence Trail** | Informal Legal | Intuitive | Informal; not a term of art |
| **Citation Chain** | Academic | Implies references | Too narrow; just citations |

### Research Evidence

**[8] Wikipedia - Chain of Custody** [reference]
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_of_custody
> "Chain of custody (CoC), in legal contexts, is the chronological documentation or paper trail that records the sequence of custody, control, transfer, analysis, and disposition of materials, physical or electronic evidence."

**[9] NIST - Chain of Evidence** [official]
https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/chain_of_evidence
> "A process and record that shows who obtained the evidence; where and when the evidence was obtained; who secured the evidence; and who had control or possession of the evidence."

**[10] AccountTools - Audit Trail** [practitioner]
https://www.accountingtools.com/articles/audit-trail
> "An audit trail is the documented flow of a transaction and is used to investigate how a source document was translated into an entry, and from there was inserted into the financial statements."

**[11] Trullion - What Is an Audit Trail** [practitioner]
https://trullion.com/blog/audit-trail-guide/
> "A paper trail, also known as an audit trail, refers to a series of documented records that trace the sequence of actions or events in financial transactions, business processes, and other activities."

**[12] Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy - Precedent and Analogy** [academic]
https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/legal-reas-prec/
> "The rationale of the case 'brief' contains a summary of the specific legal reasons given by the court in support of its decision."

**[13] AskDifference - Justification vs Rationale** [reference]
https://www.askdifference.com/justification-vs-rationale/
> "Justification involves proof that something is right or reasonable, often after the fact, whereas rationale is the logic behind decisions or actions, typically outlined beforehand."

**[14] NIH - Chain of Custody in Forensics** [academic]
https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK551677/
> "The goal is to establish that the evidence is related to the alleged crime, was collected from the scene, and was in its original/unaltered condition rather than having been tampered with or 'planted' deceitfully."

### Conflict Detection

Sources [8], [9] focus on **physical evidence integrity** (chain of custody), while sources [10], [11] focus on **transaction traceability** (audit trail). Our use case is closer to the audit trail concept - trace of a logical chain of logic - but with legal/regulatory implications.

### Recommendation

**Primary Term: `Rationale`**

Alternative consideration: `Audit Trail` (if emphasis on traceability over logic)

Rationale:
- "Rationale" directly means "the logic behind a decision" [12], [13]
- Captures the logical flow: code baseline -> exemption check -> jurisdiction derivation
- Well-understood in legal and technical contexts
- Singular noun; maps cleanly to a domain object

**Why not "Chain of Custody":**
- Implies physical evidence handling, not logical logic
- Connotation of tamper prevention is not our primary concern

**Why not "Audit Trail":**
- More about record of what happened than explanation of why
- Better suited for log/monitor systems

**Composite Option:**

If the system needs both, consider:
- `Rationale` - the logical basis for the determination
- `AuditTrail` - the chronological record of how the determination was produced

## 3. The Work Type Transform

**Question:** What do we call the translation from user language ("panel upgrade") to code language ("electrical service equipment")?

### Candidate Terms

| Term | Domain Origin | Pros | Cons |
|------|---------------|------|------|
| **Interpretation** | Legal, Linguistics | Legal precedent for statute interpretation | Ambiguous (legal vs linguistic definition) |
| **Construction** | Legal | Specific legal definition | Too technical; unfamiliar outside law |
| **Classification** | Taxonomy, Data Science | Well-understood; implies categorization | Doesn't capture the transform aspect |
| **Map** | Software, Statistics | Technical; implies correspondence | Generic; could mean many things |
| **Translation** | Linguistics | Intuitive for "language A to language B" | Implies complete equivalence |
| **Categorization** | Information Science | Process-oriented | Doesn't capture the interpretive aspect |

### Research Evidence

**[15] Cornell Law - Statutory Construction** [official]
https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/statutory_construction
> "Statutory construction, also known as statutory interpretation, is the process by which courts interpret and apply legislation."

**[16] iPleaders - Interpretation vs Construction** [academic]
https://blog.ipleaders.in/difference-between-interpretation-and-construction/
> "Interpretation refers to the activity of discovery of the linguistic definition of the statutory text, while construction refers to the activity that determines the legal effect to be given to a statutory provision."

**[17] LanguageLine - Translation vs Interpretation** [practitioner]
https://www.languageline.com/blog/explaining-the-difference-between-language-translation-and-interpretation
> "Language translation refers only to the written word, while interpretation refers to the spoken word."

**[18] Medium - Classification vs Categorization** [practitioner]
https://medium.com/@igor.berner/classification-vs-categorization-marking-vs-labeling-844dd9d6371a
> "While classification is the predefined structure - the framework of levels set up - categorization is the step where we evaluate each asset and determine which class it belongs to."

**[19] Statistics Canada - Classification Code Transform** [official]
https://www150.statcan.gc.ca/n1/pub/87-542-x/2011002/s05-eng.htm
> "The transform involves assignment of each element (such as industry, product, occupation, or classification code) to a domain and sub-domain."

**[20] NN/g - Taxonomy 101** [practitioner]
https://www.nngroup.com/articles/taxonomy-101/
> "A taxonomy is a closed list of acceptable terms that are arranged hierarchically and are used to describe and classify content."

### Convergence Signal

Sources [15], [16] establish that legal "interpretation" focuses on **linguistic definition**, while "construction" focuses on **legal effect**. Our domain involves transform of user language to code language - this is closer to interpretation than construction.

### Recommendation

**Primary Term: `Interpretation`**

Rationale:
- Legal precedent: statutory interpretation is exactly the process of what language means [15], [16]
- Our use case: what "panel upgrade" means in terms of code language
- The term "interpretation" in legal contexts implies an authoritative result, not mere guess
- Intuitive: "the system's interpretation of your work description"

**Rejected alternatives:**
- "Translation" - implies 1:1 equivalence; interpretation allows for judgment
- "Map" - too generic; doesn't convey the interpretive judgment involved
- "Classification" - implies the result is predetermined; interpretation allows for ambiguity derivation
- "Construction" - too technical; reserved for determination of legal effect, not definition

**Domain Object Name:** `WorkTypeInterpretation`

## 4. The Code Citation

**Question:** What do we call a reference to a specific code section with verbatim text?

### Candidate Terms

| Term | Domain Origin | Pros | Cons |
|------|---------------|------|------|
| **Citation** | Legal, Academic | Standard term; implies authority | Could mean just the reference, not the text |
| **Reference** | Academic | Generic and understood | Doesn't imply verbatim text |
| **Excerpt** | Publication | Implies verbatim portion | Doesn't convey the reference aspect |
| **Quote** | General | Implies verbatim | Informal; doesn't convey legal authority |
| **Provision** | Legal | Refers to the code section itself | Doesn't convey verbatim inclusion |

### Research Evidence

**[21] Georgetown Law - Cite to Statutes** [official]
https://guides.ll.georgetown.edu/bluebook/citing-statutes
> "Use a full citation the first time you cite a statute in a legal memo or brief, then use a short form for subsequent citations to that same statute."

**[22] Library of Congress - Citations for Statutes** [official]
https://guides.loc.gov/federal-statutes/citations
> "A United States Code citation includes a title number and section number-for example, 26 U.S.C. 115 refers to section 115 of title 26 of the United States Code."

**[23] Paperpal - Citations vs References** [academic]
https://paperpal.com/blog/academic-writing-guides/difference-between-citations-and-references
> "The purpose of a citation is to point to additional information whereas the purpose of a reference is to supply that additional information."

**[24] US House - Detailed Guide to USC** [official]
https://uscode.house.gov/detailed_guide.xhtml
> "When statutory text is quoted, it is quoted verbatim without translations, although editorial brackets are inserted when needed."

### Convergence Signal

Sources [21], [22], [23] consistently use "citation" as the standard term for legal references. The academic distinction [23] clarifies that citations point to information while references supply it - for our domain, we want both (the pointer AND the verbatim text).

### Recommendation

**Primary Term: `Citation`**

With structure:
```typescript
interface Citation {
  reference: CodeReference;  // e.g., "NEC 2023 110.12(A)"
  excerpt: string;           // verbatim text from the code
  source: CodeSource;        // which code edition/version
}
```

Rationale:
- "Citation" is the established legal and academic term [21], [22]
- The compound structure allows both the reference (pointer) and excerpt (text)
- Follows legal text conventions where citations include both locator and quoted text
- Intuitive: "the citation from the code"

**Rejected alternatives:**
- "Quote" - too informal; doesn't convey legal authority
- "Reference" - typically means just the pointer, not the content
- "Excerpt" - emphasizes the text but not the authoritative source

## 5. The Jurisdiction Derivation

**Question:** What do we call the process that derives applicable code versions from postal code?

### Candidate Terms

| Term | Domain Origin | Pros | Cons |
|------|---------------|------|------|
| **Jurisdiction** | Legal | Implies legal authority | Generic; could mean the area or the authority |
| **Venue** | Legal | Specific location | Wrong connotation (courts, not codes) |
| **Locality** | Government | Specific to local government | Too narrow; may not include state/federal |
| **Authority** | Government | Implies power to regulate | Ambiguous (the entity vs the concept) |
| **Locale** | Software i18n | Familiar to developers | Wrong connotation (language/culture, not law) |

### Research Evidence

**[25] Dentons - Venue vs Jurisdiction** [practitioner]
https://www.dentons.com/en/insights/newsletters/2022/april/27/consumer-finance-report/back-to-basics-continued-what-is-the-difference-between-venue-and-jurisdiction
> "Jurisdiction is the legal authority of a court to hear and decide a case... venue signifies the geographic location where a case is to be tried."

**[26] Cornell Law - Definition of Locality** [official]
https://www.law.cornell.edu/definitions/uscode.php?width=840&height=800&iframe=true&def_id=5-USC-1900805475-814924144&term_occur=999&term_src=
> "'Local' and 'locality' means any local government authority or agency or component thereof within a State with jurisdiction over matters at a county, municipal, or other local government level."

**[27] Wikipedia - Jurisdiction** [reference]
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jurisdiction
> "'Jurisdiction' is the legal term for the legal authority held by a legal entity to enact justice. More broadly, a 'jurisdiction' can be understood as a category name for any separate polity legally constituted as such."

**[28] HG.org - Federal State Local Jurisdiction** [practitioner]
https://www.hg.org/legal-articles/federal-state-or-local-who-has-jurisdiction-31065
> "Jurisdiction refers to the legal authority and power to make and enforce laws within a defined area or over specific subject matters."

### Convergence Signal

Sources [25], [27], [28] agree that "jurisdiction" refers to both the geographic area AND the legal authority. This dual definition is actually beneficial for our use case - we derive both "where" and "which codes apply."

### Recommendation

**Primary Term: `Jurisdiction`**

With a compound derivation process:
```typescript
interface Jurisdiction {
  locality: PostalAddress;           // the geographic location
  authorities: CodeAuthority[];      // which bodies have authority
  applicableCodes: CodeVersion[];    // which code versions apply
}
```

**Process Term: `JurisdictionDerivation`**

Rationale:
- "Jurisdiction" encompasses both geography and legal authority [27]
- Standard term in codes and permits: "the jurisdiction with authority" (AHJ)
- Familiar to contractors, inspectors, and homeowners
- Clear compound: "jurisdiction derivation" = "derive which jurisdiction's codes apply"

**Rejected alternatives:**
- "Venue" - wrong domain (courts, not departments)
- "Locale" - wrong connotation (i18n, not legal authority)
- "Authority" - ambiguous (could be the entity or the power)
- "Locality" - too narrow; doesn't include state/federal layers

## Cross-Domain Terminology Insights

### From Culinary Arts

**[29] Michelin Guide - Mise en Place** [practitioner]
https://guide.michelin.com/us/en/article/features/mise-en-place-cooking
> "Mise en place is a French culinary phrase that means 'all in its place' or 'gather' and refers to organization and arrangement of ingredients and equipment required to prepare menu items before you begin."

**Insight:** The culinary term "mise en place" teaches us that professional domains often coin precise terms for preparation states. Our "determination" is the equivalent of the finished dish; our "rationale" is the mise en place that made it possible.

### From Maritime Navigation

**[30] Wikipedia - Dead Reckon** [reference]
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead_reckoning
> "Dead reckon is the determination without the aid of celestial navigation of the position of a ship or aircraft from the record of the courses sailed or flown, the distance made, the known start point, and the known or estimated drift."

**Insight:** Navigation distinguishes between a "fix" (known position from external reference) and a "DR position" (calculated position). Our system similarly distinguishes between "code citation" (authoritative fix) and "interpretation" (reasoned position).

### From Standards Conformance

**[31] Applied Design - Conformance vs Compliance** [practitioner]
https://www.appliedengineering.com/blog/2021/4/9/conformance-vs-compliance-whats-the-difference
> "Compliance typically refers to adherence to mandatory legal or regulatory requirements, while conformance refers to adherence to voluntary standards or specifications."

**Insight:** Codes are mandatory compliance, not voluntary conformance. Our terminology should reflect this: a "determination" is a compliance determination, not a conformance assessment.

### From Healthcare

**[32] BMC Medicine - Clinical Pathways** [academic]
https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-016-0580-z
> "The term 'clinical pathway' is reserved for the path within a clinic, or a 24-hour department of a hospital. A care pathway is longer and includes outpatient department's activities, discharge from the hospital and after-care."

**Insight:** Healthcare distinguishes scope with different terms. We might consider: "PermitDetermination" (the immediate output) vs "PermitCompliance" (the broader lifecycle inclusive of inspection, certificate of occupancy, etc.).

### From Domain-Driven Design

**[33] Martin Fowler - Ubiquitous Language** [practitioner]
https://martinfowler.com/bliki/UbiquitousLanguage.html
> "This language should be based on the Domain Model used in the software - hence the need for it to be rigorous, since software doesn't cope well with ambiguity."

**[34] Enterprise Craftsmanship - Ubiquitous Language** [practitioner]
https://enterprisecraftsmanship.com/posts/ubiquitous-language-naming/
> "Any unnecessary or excessive words would result in communication overhead because chances are, business people don't use those words when spoken about the problem domain."

**Insight:** Our terminology should match what contractors, homeowners, and officials already say. "Determination" is used by departments. "Permit required" is what homeowners ask. "Code citation" is what inspectors reference.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Warned Against by Sources

**[34] Enterprise Craftsmanship** warns:
> "A class named 'FurnitureEntity' is not named properly. No business person calls a piece of furniture 'FurnitureEntity'."

**Application:** Don't call it `PermitRequirementDeterminationResult`. Call it `Determination`.

**[7] Encyclopedia.com** distinguishes:
> "Assessment is often used as a preliminary step before a diagnosis."

**Application:** Don't call the final output an "assessment" - that implies preliminary, not conclusive.

**[25] Dentons** clarifies:
> "Venue should be thought of as a subset of jurisdiction."

**Application:** Don't conflate venue (specific courthouse) with jurisdiction (legal authority). Permits are about jurisdiction, not venue.

## Proposed Ubiquitous Language

### Domain Objects

| Domain Object | Recommended Term | Rationale |
|---------------|------------------|-----------|
| Permit requirement determination result | `Determination` | Government standard; implies authority and finality |
| Proof chain that supports determination | `Rationale` | Legal term for logic; captures logical flow |
| Work type transform | `Interpretation` | Legal precedent; implies authoritative result |
| Code citation with verbatim text | `Citation` | Academic/legal standard; understood universally |
| Jurisdiction derivation | `Jurisdiction` | Encompasses geography and legal authority |

### Support Terms

| Concept | Term | Usage |
|---------|------|-------|
| The code section reference | `Reference` | "NEC 2023 Section 110.12" |
| The verbatim code text | `Excerpt` | The quoted portion of the code |
| The postal-to-codes derivation | `JurisdictionDerivation` | Process to find applicable codes |
| The applicable code version | `CodeVersion` | Specific edition of a code (e.g., "NEC 2023") |
| The authority with jurisdiction | `Authority` | The body that enforces the code |

### Full Object Model

```
Determination
├── result: PermitRequired | PermitNotRequired | Uncertain
├── confidence: High | Medium | Low
├── jurisdiction: Jurisdiction
│   ├── locality: PostalAddress
│   ├── authorities: Authority[]
│   └── applicableCodes: CodeVersion[]
├── interpretation: Interpretation
│   ├── userDescription: string ("panel upgrade")
│   └── codeClassification: string ("electrical service equipment")
├── rationale: Rationale
│   ├── citations: Citation[]
│   │   ├── reference: CodeReference
│   │   └── excerpt: string
│   ├── exemptionsChecked: Exemption[]
│   └── chain: LogicStep[]
└── timestamp: ISO8601
```

## Conclusion

The recommended ubiquitous language prioritizes:

1. **Government alignment** - Terms like "determination," "jurisdiction," and "citation" match how departments, the IRS, EPA, and SSA already communicate
2. **Legal precision** - Terms like "interpretation" and "rationale" have specific legal definitions that map to our use cases
3. **Domain-driven design principles** - Terms are what practitioners actually say, not programmer jargon
4. **Minimal ambiguity** - Each term has a clear, singular definition in context

This terminology should enable clear communication between:
- Homeowners who ask "Do I need a permit?"
- Contractors who explain their scope of work
- Officials who make determinations
- Software engineers who implement the system
