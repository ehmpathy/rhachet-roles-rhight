research on templates available for the patenter role workflow system.

## summary

this research covers templates for patent application documents, claim drafting, CLI/workflow systems, and project scaffolding. 25 sources were found across official USPTO documentation, academic templates, practitioner resources, and tutorial guides.

key findings:
1. **LaTeX patent templates exist** — but post-2024 USPTO mandates DOCX format for specifications
2. **37 CFR 1.77** — canonical section ordering for patent applications
3. **claim structure** — preamble + transitional phrase + body pattern is universal
4. **stone/guard route pattern** — sequential phase workflow from rhachet applies directly
5. **anti-patterns well documented** — inconsistent terminology, means-plus-function terms, missing antecedent basis

## USPTO formal requirements

### 37 CFR 1.77 — arrangement of application elements

> "The specification should include these sections in order: (1) Title, (2) Cross-reference to related applications, (3) Federally sponsored research statement, (4) Joint research agreement parties, (5) Incorporation by reference, (6) Prior disclosures statement, (7) Background, (8) Brief summary, (9) Brief description of drawings, (10) Detailed description, (11) Claims, (12) Abstract, (13) Sequence Listing" [5]

**format**: section headings in UPPERCASE, no bold or underline
**empty sections**: use "Not Applicable"
**pagination**: consecutive page numbering required
**credibility**: [official] — current regulation (November 2024 MPEP update)

### USPTO DOCX requirement (2024)

> "Beginning January 17, 2024, the description, claims, and abstract for a nonprovisional utility patent application specification must all be filed in DOCX format to avoid a non-DOCX surcharge of $430 (large), $172 (small), $86 (micro)." [6]

**impact on LaTeX**: must convert to DOCX for filing; PDF acceptable only for drawings
**credibility**: [official] — USPTO guidance

### MPEP 608.01(a) — arrangement of application

> "The order of arrangement of specification elements as set forth in 37 CFR 1.77(b) is preferable (though not required) in framing the nonprovisional specification." [7]

**note**: order is "preferable" not mandatory, but following it reduces examiner friction
**credibility**: [official] — MPEP

### USPTO patent format sample

> "Top margin at least 2 cm (3/4 inch), left-side margin at least 2.5 cm (1 inch), right-side margin at least 2 cm (3/4 inch), bottom margin at least 2 cm (3/4 inch). Font preferably 12 point Courier." [8]

**pagination**: consecutive Arabic numerals, centrally located
**credibility**: [official] — USPTO sample document

## patent claim templates

### USPTO claim drafting workshop

> "A patent claim has three parts: preamble, transitional phrase, and body. Each claim is a single sentence that begins with a capital letter and ends with a period." [9]

**preamble**: provides context for the invention
**transitional phrase**: "comprising" used in 99% of claims (open-ended)
**body**: lists features as single sentence
**credibility**: [official] — USPTO training materials (2021)

### WIPO patent claim format guide

> "An independent claim is a stand alone claim that contains all the subject matter necessary to define the invention. A dependent claim refers to a previous claim and specifies a further limitation." [10]

**independent claims**: complete definition
**dependent claims**: incorporates by reference + adds limitation
**numbering**: consecutively in ascending order
**credibility**: [official] — WIPO guidance (2018)

### BESTPATENT algorithm/AI claim template

> "For ML inventions: (1) Computer-implemented method claim, (2) Computer program claim, (3) Device claim, (4) Storage medium claim, (5) Operating method claim, (6) Training method claim, (7) Use cases claim." [11]

**ML-specific pattern**: separate training vs operating method claims
**coverage strategy**: multiple independent claims for pipeline coverage
**use claim pattern**: "Use of the method of claim X for..."
**credibility**: [practitioner] — European patent firm (2022)

## anti-patterns (what NOT to do)

### IPWatchdog — ten claim drafting mistakes

> "Inconsistent claim terms can result in unintended consequences during patent litigation. If a motor is recited, you should not refer to it with different names. Adding additional details when referring back ('the plastic gear' instead of 'the gear') adds unintended limitations." [12]

**anti-patterns identified**:
- inconsistent terminology across claims
- claiming results instead of structures
- lack of antecedent basis ("said lever" without prior introduction)
**credibility**: [practitioner] — IPWatchdog (October 2021)

### WIPO common pitfalls

> "When patent attorneys must reverse-engineer the invention from incomplete inputs, drafting becomes guesswork, which leads to rejections. Drafting in isolation without fully considering known prior art is one of the most preventable mistakes." [13]

**anti-patterns identified**:
- incomplete inventor input to drafter
- ignoring prior art during claim drafting
- overly broad OR overly narrow claims
**credibility**: [official] — WIPO (2019)

### Sagacious IP — claim drafting mistakes

> "Avoid characterization as means-plus-function format by refraining from non-structural terms: Module, Component, Unit, Element, Mechanism, System, Apparatus." [14]

**anti-patterns identified**:
- vague structural terms trigger narrow 112(f) interpretation
- missing transitional phrases
- grammar violations (multiple sentences, wrong articles)
**credibility**: [practitioner] — patent research firm

## patent abstract requirements

### USPTO MPEP 608.01(b) — abstract

> "The abstract shall be as concise as the disclosure permits (preferably 50 to 150 words). The abstract must commence on a separate sheet, preferably following the claims." [15]

**word count**: 50-150 words optimal
**format**: single paragraph, narrative form
**content**: points out what is NEW in the art
**credibility**: [official] — MPEP (November 2024)

### WIPO abstract guidelines

> "The abstract shall not contain statements on the alleged merits or value of the claimed invention or on its speculative application." [16]

**anti-patterns**:
- no "revolutionary" or "novel" claims
- no comparisons to prior art
- no marketing language
**credibility**: [official] — WIPO standard

## conflict detection

### LaTeX vs DOCX format conflict

sources [1-4] describe LaTeX templates for patent applications, while source [6] mandates DOCX format for USPTO submissions starting January 2024. **recommendation**: LaTeX workflows now require pandoc or manual conversion step; consider generating DOCX directly.

### 37 CFR 1.77 section order enforcement

source [7] notes the order is "preferable" not mandatory, while sources [5], [6], [8] present as standard. **recommendation**: follow the order to reduce examiner friction.

### Cookiecutter vs Copier template engines

source [22] argues Copier is superior for maintained projects due to update support; source [19] uses Cookiecutter. **recommendation**: use Copier if template will evolve; Cookiecutter for static one-time generation.

## recommendations for patenter

### document generation

1. **follow 37 CFR 1.77 ordering** — the canonical section sequence
2. **generate DOCX output** — mandatory for USPTO submission post-2024
3. **implement claim templates** — preamble/transition/body structure with validation
4. **abstract word count validation** — enforce 50-150 word limit

### workflow structure

1. **adopt stone/guard pattern** — from rhachet for sequential phases
2. **add validation guards** — terminology consistency, word count, antecedent basis
3. **use Copier for templates** — supports template evolution and updates

### anti-pattern prevention

1. **terminology consistency checker** — flag inconsistent claim terms
2. **means-plus-function detector** — warn on Module/Component/Unit/Element/Mechanism/System/Apparatus
3. **antecedent basis validator** — ensure "said X" has prior introduction
