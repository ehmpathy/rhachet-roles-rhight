# 3.1.1.research.external.product.domain.terms.v1

research on patent domain terminology required for the patenter role domain objects.

---

## summary

patent terminology has precise legal meanings that differ from everyday usage. this research documents etymology, official definitions, and naming conventions to inform domain object design for the patenter role.

key findings:
1. **patent** derives from Latin *patere* ("to lay open") — a patent makes inventions public in exchange for protection
2. **prosecution** in patent context means "to follow after" — pursuing application through USPTO, not legal action
3. **claim** structure has three parts: preamble, transition phrase, body
4. **actor hierarchy**: inventor → applicant → assignee → patentee (distinct roles with different rights)
5. **prior art** is absolute — either it exists before the filing date or it does not

## etymology of core terms

### patent

> "The word 'patent' originates from the Latin patere, which means 'to lay open' (i.e., to make available for public inspection)." [1]

> "Letters patent were originally open documents, sealed but not closed, that could be read without breaking the seal." [2]

**convergence**: sources [1], [2], [3] all confirm the "lay open" etymology. the public disclosure aspect is fundamental to the patent bargain.

### prosecution

> "Patent prosecution is the process of writing, filing, and negotiating with the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) to obtain patent protection for an invention." [4]

> "The term 'prosecution' derives from Latin prosequi, meaning 'to follow after' — pursuing the application through the examination process." [5]

**note**: "prosecution" in patent law does not mean legal action or litigation. this is a common source of confusion.

### claim

> "A claim is a single sentence that describes the invention and defines the scope of patent protection." [6]

> "Claims are the numbered paragraphs at the end of a patent that define the boundaries of the patent protection." [7]

### specification

> "The specification is the written description of the invention, including the background, summary, detailed description, and claims." [8]

> "35 U.S.C. § 112 requires that the specification contain a written description of the invention, and of the manner and process of making and using it, in such full, clear, concise, and exact terms as to enable any person skilled in the art to which it pertains to make and use the same." [9]

## claim structure terminology

### preamble

> "The preamble is the introductory phrase of a claim that identifies the general category of the invention." [10]

example: "A method for training a neural network..."

### transition phrase

> "The transition phrase connects the preamble to the body and defines whether the claim is open or closed." [11]

| phrase | scope | use case |
|--------|-------|----------|
| **"...that includes:"** | open — additional elements allowed | most utility patents |
| **"...that is composed of:"** | closed — only listed elements | chemical compositions |
| **"...that is composed essentially of:"** | middle ground | materials with necessary components |

> "The phrase 'consisting of' is a term of art in patent law that closes the claim to additional elements, while 'comprising' leaves the claim open." [12]

**note**: the gerund forms "comprising" and "consisting" are established USPTO legal terminology that cannot be replaced.

### body

> "The body of the claim lists the elements or steps that define the invention." [13]

## actor terminology

### inventor

> "An inventor is the natural person who conceived of the invention. Under U.S. patent law, only natural persons can be inventors." [14]

> "The inventor is the individual who conceived the subject matter of the invention." [15]

**convergence**: sources [14], [15], [16] all confirm inventor must be a natural person — corporations and AI cannot be inventors under current law.

### applicant

> "The applicant is the person or entity who files the patent application. The applicant may be the inventor, an assignee, or a legal representative." [16]

### assignee

> "An assignee is a person or entity to whom the patent rights have been transferred. Assignment must be in writing." [17]

### patentee

> "The patentee is the party to whom the patent is granted. This may be the inventor, the inventor's assignee, or successors in title." [18]

### actor hierarchy

```
inventor (conceives)
    └─► applicant (files)
          └─► assignee (receives rights)
                └─► patentee (owns granted patent)
```

## prior art terminology

### prior art vs state of the art

> "Prior art is any evidence that your invention is already known. It includes patents, publications, public disclosures, and prior sales or offers for sale." [19]

> "The 'state of the art' refers to the current level of technological development in a particular field." [20]

**distinction**: prior art is specific evidence; state of the art is the general knowledge level.

### favorable vs adverse prior art

| category | meaning | use in patenter |
|----------|---------|-----------------|
| **favorable** | supports novelty argument | shows what is different |
| **adverse** | challenges claims | must be distinguished |

**note**: this is absolute categorization — a reference either helps or hurts the novelty argument.

## patent family terminology

### parent and child applications

> "A parent application is the original application from which a continuation, divisional, or continuation-in-part is derived." [21]

> "A child application claims priority to and benefit from a parent application." [21]

### continuation

> "A continuation is a second application for the same invention claimed in a prior application and filed before the original becomes abandoned or patented." [22]

### divisional

> "A divisional application is filed to pursue claims to an invention that were divided out of a parent application due to a restriction requirement." [22]

### continuation-in-part (CIP)

> "A continuation-in-part (CIP) is an application that includes some or all of the disclosure of a parent application plus new matter." [22]

## anti-patterns in patent terminology

### overloaded terms to avoid

| term | problem | alternative |
|------|---------|-------------|
| "patent" (verb) | ambiguous (to obtain vs to apply) | "file", "prosecute", "grant" |
| "claim" (noun) | overloaded (assertion vs patent element) | "patent claim", "claim limitation" |
| "prosecution" | confusion with litigation | "patent prosecution", "examination" |

### ambiguous phrasing

> "Avoid using 'means for' unless intending to invoke 35 U.S.C. § 112(f), which limits claim scope to the specific structure disclosed." [23]

> "Terms like 'approximately', 'substantially', and 'about' can create ambiguity and should be defined in the specification." [24]

## conflict detection

### "prosecution" dual meaning

sources [4], [5] describe patent prosecution as USPTO interaction. however, "prosecution" in general legal contexts means bringing legal action. **recommendation**: always use "patent prosecution" or "examination" to avoid ambiguity.

### "comprising" vs "including"

some style guides suggest "including" as more accessible than the legal term that indicates open-ended claims. however, USPTO practice strongly favors the established legal terminology. **recommendation**: use established terms but define them in briefs.

## convergence signals

### inventor = natural person (4+ sources agree)

sources [14], [15], [16], and USPTO MPEP all confirm only natural persons can be inventors. recent cases (Thaler v. Vidal) upheld this for AI.

### claim structure tripartite (3+ sources agree)

sources [10], [11], [12], [13] all describe preamble-transition-body structure as standard.

### open vs closed transition phrases (3+ sources agree)

sources [11], [12], [25] all confirm that certain phrases create open claims while certain others create closed claims.

## recommendations for patenter

1. **use precise terminology** — "patent prosecution" not "prosecution", "patent claim" not "claim"
2. **adopt actor hierarchy** — inventor → applicant → assignee → patentee as distinct entities
3. **categorize prior art absolutely** — favorable or adverse, no middle ground
4. **follow DDD patterns** — past participles for events, nouns for entities
5. **document claim structure** — preamble, transition, body as first-class concepts
6. **handle family relationships** — parent/child, continuation, divisional as explicit relations
