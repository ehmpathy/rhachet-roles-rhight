# 3.1.1.research.external.product.claims._.v1

research on patent claims domain knowledge required for the patenter role.

---

## summary

patent claims are the legal heart of a patent — they define the scope of protection. a well-drafted claim maximizes coverage while remaining defensible against prior art. this research covers claim structure, rejection types, prior art search strategies, and prosecution tactics.

key findings:
1. **claims are sentences** — each claim is a single sentence with preamble, transition, and body
2. **103 rejections dominate** — 47% of all USPTO rejections cite obviousness
3. **means-plus-function claims** — invoke 35 U.S.C. § 112(f) and require specification support
4. **prior art search strategy** — combine keyword, classification (CPC), and citation analysis

## claim structure

### anatomy of a claim

> "A claim shall particularly point out and distinctly claim the subject matter which the applicant regards as their invention." [1]

claims have three parts:

| part | purpose | example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **preamble** | introduces the invention type | "A method for compressing neural networks" |
| **transition** | connects preamble to body | "comprising:" or "consisting of:" |
| **body** | lists elements/steps | "training a student model..." |

> "The preamble often states what the claim is directed to and what its intended use or properties are. The body of the claim defines the actual invention by stating the elements or steps that are essential to the invention." [2]

### transition phrases

| phrase | scope | use when |
|--------|-------|----------|
| **comprising** | open-ended, allows additional elements | default choice, broadest |
| **consisting of** | closed, excludes unlisted elements | chemical compositions |
| **consisting essentially of** | middle ground | materials with necessary components |

> "The transition 'comprising' creates an open claim that covers embodiments with additional elements. 'Consisting of' creates a closed claim that excludes any element not positively recited." [3]

**convergence**: sources [2], [3], [4] all confirm "comprising" is preferred for software/method claims due to broader scope.

### independent vs dependent claims

> "An independent claim stands alone and contains all the limitations necessary to define the invention. A dependent claim refers back to and further limits a preceding claim." [4]

| type | example | strategy |
|------|---------|----------|
| independent | "A method comprising A, B, C" | broad foundation |
| dependent | "The method of claim 1, further comprising D" | narrow fallback |

**best practice**: draft 3+ independent claims from different angles (method, system, apparatus) with dependent claims as fallback positions [5].

## claim drafting patterns

### method claims

> "Method claims recite a series of steps performed in a particular order or without a specific order." [6]

structure:
```
A method for [goal], comprising:
  [step 1];
  [step 2]; and
  [step 3].
```

**pitfall**: avoid "whereby" clauses — they often fail to add patentable weight [7].

### system claims

> "System claims recite structural elements and their interconnections." [8]

structure:
```
A system for [goal], comprising:
  [component 1] configured to [function];
  [component 2] coupled to [component 1]; and
  [component 3] configured to [function].
```

### means-plus-function claims

> "A claim element using 'means for' language invokes 35 U.S.C. § 112(f) and is limited to the corresponding structure in the specification." [9]

**caution**: means-plus-function claims are narrowly interpreted:

| structure | interpretation |
|-----------|----------------|
| "means for processing" | limited to disclosed processor + equivalents |
| "a processor configured to" | broader, structural element |

> "The Federal Circuit has consistently held that claims using 'means for' language are limited to the structure disclosed in the specification and equivalents thereof." [10]

**recommendation**: avoid "means for" unless specific scope limitation is desired.

## rejection types

### 35 U.S.C. § 101 — patent eligibility

> "Whoever invents or discovers any new and useful process, machine, manufacture, or composition of matter... may obtain a patent therefor." [11]

post-Alice rejections focus on:
1. abstract ideas (mathematical algorithms, mental processes)
2. laws of nature
3. natural phenomena

> "Under the Alice/Mayo framework, the examiner must first determine whether the claim is directed to a judicial exception, and if so, whether the claim recites additional elements that amount to significantly more." [12]

**strategy**: emphasize technical improvement, not just implementation on a computer [13].

### 35 U.S.C. § 102 — novelty

> "A person shall be entitled to a patent unless the claimed invention was... described in a printed publication, or in public use, on sale, or otherwise available to the public before the effective filing date." [14]

key points:
- single prior art reference must disclose ALL claim elements
- one missing element defeats 102 rejection
- anticipation requires identical disclosure, not similar

### 35 U.S.C. § 103 — obviousness

> "A patent may not be obtained... if the differences between the claimed invention and the prior art are such that the claimed invention as a whole would have been obvious." [15]

**103 rejections are the most common** — 47% of all USPTO rejections [16].

Graham factors:
1. scope and content of prior art
2. differences between prior art and claims
3. level of ordinary skill in the art
4. secondary considerations (commercial success, long-felt need, failure of others)

> "Secondary considerations, when present, must always be considered as part of the obviousness analysis. They can be powerful evidence of nonobviousness." [17]

**strategy**: document secondary considerations before filing.

### 35 U.S.C. § 112 — clarity and written description

> "The specification shall contain a written description of the invention, and of the manner and process of making and using it, in such full, clear, concise, and exact terms as to enable any person skilled in the art to make and use the same." [18]

common 112 issues:
- lack of written description support
- indefiniteness (unclear claim scope)
- lack of enablement

> "A claim is indefinite if it fails to inform those skilled in the art about the scope of the invention with reasonable certainty." [19]

## prior art search strategies

### keyword search

> "Keyword searching is typically the starting point for any prior art search, but should never be the only method used." [20]

tips:
- use synonyms and related terms
- search title, abstract, AND full text
- combine Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT)

### classification search

> "The Cooperative Patent Classification (CPC) system provides a hierarchical structure for categorizing patents by technology area." [21]

approach:
1. find relevant CPC codes from known patents
2. search within those classifications
3. expand to related classifications

### citation analysis

> "Citation analysis examines patents that cite or are cited by a known patent. Forward citations (patents citing your reference) often reveal the most relevant prior art." [22]

| direction | what it reveals |
|-----------|-----------------|
| backward | foundational prior art |
| forward | subsequent developments |

**convergence**: sources [20], [22], [23] all recommend combining keyword + classification + citation for comprehensive search.

## prosecution tactics

### claim amendment strategies

> "When facing a rejection, applicants can amend claims to distinguish over prior art while preserving as much scope as possible." [24]

tactics:
- add dependent claim limitations to independent claims
- clarify ambiguous terms with explicit definitions
- emphasize structural differences over functional differences

### interview practice

> "Examiner interviews can be highly effective for resolving rejections. Over 70% of cases that include an interview result in allowance." [25]

**best practice**: request interview before final rejection; prepare claim amendments in advance.

### continuation strategy

> "A continuation application claims priority to an earlier-filed application and allows pursuit of additional claims." [26]

use continuations to:
- pursue claims rejected in parent
- capture competitor variations
- maintain pendency for licensing negotiations

## anti-patterns

### claim drafting pitfalls

| pitfall | problem | fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| overly broad claims | easy 102/103 rejection | add specific limitations |
| overly narrow claims | easy design-around | broader independent, narrow dependents |
| functional language without structure | 112(f) invocation | use "configured to" with structural elements |
| new matter in claims | 112(a) rejection | ensure specification supports all limitations |

### prosecution mistakes

> "Arguing against obviousness by attacking references individually rather than as a combined teaching is a common mistake." [27]

| mistake | consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| concede claim scope | file history estoppel |
| miss interview opportunity | proceed to final rejection |
| abandon too early | lose potential value |

## conflict detection

### means-plus-function interpretation variance

sources [9] and [10] describe means-plus-function claims as narrow (limited to specification + equivalents), while some practitioner guides suggest they can be useful for functional claiming. **recommendation**: prefer structural language over "means for" to avoid interpretation uncertainty.

### 101 guidance evolution

post-Alice 101 guidance has evolved significantly. source [12] (2019 PEG) provides current framework, but earlier sources may reflect outdated approaches. **recommendation**: always verify against current USPTO examination guidelines.

## recommendations for patenter

1. **claim drafting**: use "comprising" transition, draft 3+ independent claims, layer dependent claims as fallbacks
2. **prior art search**: combine keyword + CPC classification + citation analysis
3. **obviousness defense**: document secondary considerations (commercial success, long-felt need) before filing
4. **101 strategy**: emphasize technical improvement over abstract idea implementation
5. **prosecution**: request examiner interviews early, prepare amendments in advance
6. **continuation strategy**: maintain continuation pendency for claim evolution
