# vision: patenter role

## the outcome world

### day-in-the-life with patenter

**before**: an inventor has a novel idea. they spend weeks manually searching google patents, reading dense legal documents, trying to understand claim structures, worrying about what prior art might invalidate their claims, and struggling to write in patent-speak. they hire expensive patent attorneys who charge $15-30k per application, yet still feel uncertain about their claims' strength.

**after**: the inventor runs `rhx patent.propose`, describes their idea in plain english, and the patenter guides them through a structured route. it searches prior art automatically, surfaces relevant patents, helps them understand what's novel vs what's been done, produces a patentability analysis with draft claims, and prepares materials for attorney review or provisional file.

### the "aha" moment

when the inventor sees the `3.2.distill.claims.patentable.stone` output — a clear separation of "here's what's truly novel about your idea" vs "here's what prior art already covers" — that's when it clicks. they're not just filing paperwork; they're building a defensible intellectual property position with full awareness of the landscape.

### timeline

phases of the route (times vary by invention complexity):

1. **idea capture** (0.idea.md) — inventor describes the invention via structured template
2. **prior art research** (3.1 stones) — systematic search, fetch, categorize
3. **claims distillation** (3.2 stones) — analyze novelty, draft claims, prep for objections
4. **blueprint & delivery** (3.3, 5.1) — structure and produce formal application

note: faster than traditional attorney-led process, but actual duration depends on invention scope and prior art landscape.

## mental model

### target user

**primary**: technical founders and indie inventors who:
- are comfortable with CLI tools
- want to understand the patent landscape before spending on attorneys
- need prior art research and patentability analysis
- may draft provisional applications themselves

**secondary**: patent attorneys who want research automation (future scope)

**not for**: non-technical inventors who need hand-held GUI experiences

### how users describe it

> "it's like having a patent researcher in my terminal who does all the legwork, helps me understand what's actually novel about my idea, and prepares everything for attorney review."

## evaluation

### how well does it solve the goals?

| goal | coverage | notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| research prior art | high | automated search + fetch + categorization |
| distill proposals | high | structured claims analysis |
| devil's advocate | medium | office action prep, but limited to text analysis |
| strategy | medium | anticipates common objections, but not legal advice |

### pros

- **faster** than manual research (actual speed depends on invention complexity)
- **structured thinking** — the route forces systematic analysis
- **transparent reasoning** — every claim tied to prior art analysis
- **reproducible** — can re-run research as landscape changes
- **accessible** — indie inventors can do what used to require $20k attorneys

### cons

- **not legal advice** — still need attorney review for filing
- **search quality depends on query** — garbage in, garbage out
- **patent databases have coverage gaps** — international patents, recent filings
- **claims drafting is hard** — even with help, the language is arcane

### pit of success

| edgecase | how the route handles it |
|----------|--------------------------|
| inventor thinks everything is novel | prior art research stone forces confrontation with reality |
| claims too broad | office action prep anticipates 102/103 rejections |
| claims too narrow | blueprint stone checks claim coverage vs invention scope |
| missed prior art | multiple search passes with different query phrasings |

## open questions & assumptions

### assumptions (to verify before build)

1. **patent search API exists** — google patents, USPTO, or espacenet provides usable search API [research]
2. **text extraction reliable** — pdf/text extraction from patents works for claims sections [research]
3. **inventors can describe ideas** — mitigated by: provide structured 0.idea.md template with guided prompts [answered]
4. **output format acceptable** — latex specified in wish ("5.1.deliver.patent.latex.stone"); may need markdown → pdf as alternative for some users [answered: latex is the primary format; alternatives are future scope]

### questions to validate with wisher

1. **scope of "patent"** — utility patents only? or also design, provisional, PCT? [answered: utility patents as primary scope]
   - **utility patents**: the wish describes "claims", "office actions", "prior art research" — all utility patent concepts. this is the core scope.
   - **provisional applications**: establishes priority date with minimal documentation. could support as a "lite" route (skip office action prep, simpler output). recommend: phase 2.
   - **design patents**: protect appearance, not function. one claim, drawings-focused. different enough to warrant separate route. recommend: future scope.
   - **PCT**: international mechanism, not a patent type. extends utility/design to other jurisdictions. recommend: future scope after jurisdiction research.
2. **target jurisdiction** — USPTO focus? or international (EPO, WIPO)? [answered: USPTO for now]
   - USPTO has well-documented APIs and formats
   - international support (EPO, WIPO, PCT) can be added later once USPTO flow is solid
3. **integration** — should skills call external APIs? [answered: yes, wish specifies "skill to search", "skill to grab the pdf", and "fetches all patent contents" — external API calls are inherent to the design]
4. **output format** — latex specifically? or also word/pdf? [answered: latex as specified in wish ("5.1.deliver.patent.latex.stone"); alternatives can be added later if needed]
5. **attorney handoff** — what format do patent attorneys expect to receive? [research]

### external research needed

1. **patent database APIs** — what's available? google patents, USPTO bulk data, espacenet? [research]
2. **claim structure patterns** — what makes claims strong vs weak? [research]
3. **office action patterns** — what are common 101/102/103/112 rejections? [research]
4. **latex patent templates** — do standard templates exist? [research]
5. **API costs** — what are rate limits and pricing for patent APIs? [research]

## what is awkward?

### what feels off

1. **"patenter" as name** — is this clear? [answered: wish explicitly says "a new role, called 'patenter'" — name is established; alternatives can be considered in future iterations if feedback warrants]
2. **route stone ordering** — 0, 1, 3.1, 3.2, 3.3, 5.1 — the gaps (no 2, 4) feel odd. intentional? [answered: wish specifies these stones directly; criteria (2.x) and roadmap (4.x) phases may be added if needed during build]
3. **single route for all patent types** — provisional vs utility vs design have different needs [answered: single route for utility; provisionals and design are future scope per scope decision above]

### where design fights mental model

1. **"approvals" vs "rejections"** for prior art — these terms suggest the prior art itself is being judged, but actually we're categorizing whether it *helps or hurts* our novelty argument. recommend: "favorable" vs "adverse" [answered: recommend rename]
2. **fetch by exid** — users think in terms of titles/topics, not patent numbers. search-first is the natural flow. [answered: design is correct, search precedes fetch]

### uncomfortable tradeoffs

1. **automation vs accuracy** — more automation means more risk of missing nuance [answered: accepted tradeoff, mitigate via "not legal advice" disclaimer]
2. **accessibility vs expertise** — making patents "easy" might encourage weak filings [answered: target technical users who understand limitations]
3. **cost vs coverage** — good patent APIs aren't free; how do we handle rate limits / costs? [research: determine actual costs]
